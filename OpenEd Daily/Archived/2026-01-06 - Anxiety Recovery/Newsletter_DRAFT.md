## THE GETTING BY TRAP

The morning Lisa decided to pull her 14-year-old out of school, Maya was vomiting from anxiety. Again.

On paper, Maya wasn't failing. She had dyslexia, dyscalculia, and ADHD - but she was "getting by." Report cards didn't raise red flags. No teacher expressed concern.

But the cost of getting by had become brutal. Daily nausea. Morning tears. A nervous system in complete burnout before she'd even hit ninth grade.

"[The school system didn't identify any of this](https://www.huffpost.com/entry/alternative-educational-models-neurodivergent-kids_n_69332ad5e4b06a47b6d3db7a)," Lisa wrote in HuffPost. "I had to follow my gut, chase diagnoses, and fight for answers while she smiled and masked her way through every school day."

Today Maya is learning first aid, training as a barista, and asking "what's next?" The daily stomach aches are gone.

**"Maya is not lazy. She's not broken. She's a divergent thinker in a rigid model."**

The system might not be the problem for every child. But it might not be built for yours.

## THE 8-YEAR EXPERIMENT

A kid started piano at age eight. He was okay. Didn't practice much. Switched to guitar. Okay at that too. Then ukulele. Then back to piano.

For eight years, this kid showed up to lessons and didn't do much at home. By traditional standards, his parents wasted thousands of dollars.

Then at 16, he walked in and said: "I bought a bass."

Something clicked. The patterns matched his brain. He started practicing hours a day on his own. He had found his thing.

Deborah Pratt, who runs a music school with 150+ students, [shared this story on our podcast](https://opened.co/blog?type=podcast). She calls it the Practice Paradox.

"If I had been on him about practicing," she said, "or if his parents had required practice to have lessons, he would never have reached this."

He needed the container of music to exist in his life long enough for him to mature into it.

**Stop worrying about the daily practice chart. Worry about the survival of the habit.**
