### EPISODE DESCRIPTION
Most parents pull their kids out of music lessons the moment the "practice battles" start. It feels like a waste of money to pay for lessons when the kid isn't doing the work at home. But Deborah Pratt, founder of Southern Oregon School of Music, argues that this efficiency mindset is actually robbing children of the real ROI.

In this episode, she shares the story of a student who spent 8 years "mediocrely" playing piano, guitar, and ukulele—only to discover at age 16 that he was a born bass player. Had he quit during the "low practice" years, he never would have found it. Deb reveals why the *environment* of music (the community, the brain development, the weekly touchpoint) matters more than the daily grind, and why the "Corpus Callosum" might be the best reason to keep paying for lessons even when your kid won't practice.

## REVISED BLOG POST DRAFT

# The "Wasted" Years of Music Lessons (And Why They Matter)

**Ela Richmond**

---

We have a transactional view of extracurriculars: **Input (Money/Lessons) + Input (Practice) = Output (Skill).**

If one part of the equation breaks—if the kid stops practicing—we assume the output is broken, so we stop the input (the lessons). "Why pay for piano if he's not going to practice?"

It feels like responsible parenting. It feels like teaching accountability.

But **Deborah Pratt**, who has taught music for over 30 years and runs a school with 172 students, says this logic is fundamentally flawed. She calls it the **Practice Paradox**.

## The Parable of the Bass Player

Deb shares a story that completely upends the "Quit if they don't Practice" rule.

She had a student who started piano at age eight. He was... okay. He didn't practice much.
Then he switched to guitar. He was okay at that too.
Then ukulele.
Then *back* to piano because he realized he needed to read notes better.

For **eight years**, this kid showed up to lessons, hung out, played a bit, and didn't do much at home. By traditional standards, his parents wasted thousands of dollars.

Then, at age 16, he walked in and said: **"I bought a bass."**

Something clicked. The patterns of the bass matched his brain. He started practicing hours a day on his own. He had found *his* thing.

"If I had been on him about practicing," Deb says, "or if his parents had required him to practice to have lessons, **he would never have reached this.**"

He needed the **Container of Music** to exist in his life long enough for him to mature into it.

## The "Container" Theory of Lessons

We tend to view music lessons as "Instrument Training." Deb views them as **Brain & Identity Training**.

Even without daily practice, the weekly act of:
1.  Sitting with a mentor (Deb emphasizes the relationship aspect).
2.  Reading a new language (notation).
3.  Physically coordinating hands and eyes.

...is doing something to the brain that nothing else does.

**The Neuroscience of "Just Showing Up"**
Deb points to the **Corpus Callosum**—the bridge of nerve fibers connecting the left and right brain hemispheres. Research shows this structure is [significantly larger in musicians](https://www.jneurosci.org/content/33/3/1282).

This isn't just about playing "Hot Cross Buns." It's about building a super-highway in the brain that helps with:
*   **Emotional Regulation:** (Teenagers playing guitar to process angst).
*   **Math Skills:** (Rhythm is physical math).
*   **Executive Function:** (Tracking multiple inputs at once).

If you pull the kid out because they aren't "mastering" the songs fast enough, you lose the brain benefits too.

## When to Push vs. When to Pivot

So, do you just let them slack off forever? No. Deb offers a nuance:

**1. The "Frustration Spike" (Don't Quit Here)**
If they want to quit because it's *hard* (new hand position, harder song), **do not let them.** This is the grit-building moment. Push them through the spike.

**2. The "Interest Lull" (Pivot Here)**
If they are bored or disengaged, don't quit music—**pivot the instrument** or **pivot the expectation**.
*   *Switch to drums.*
*   *Switch to "just for fun" mode.*
*   *Switch to "social" music (choir/band).*

## The Long Game

Deb's own daughter quit trumpet in high school because Deb (the music teacher mom) had a "Practice or Quit" rule. Decades later, her daughter told her: *"I wish you'd let me continue... I could be playing today."*

The goal isn't to raise a concert pianist. It's to raise a human who feels comfortable with music, who has a larger corpus callosum, and who knows that if they stick around long enough, they might just find their bass guitar.

**The Takeaway:**
Stop worrying about the daily practice chart. Worry about the **survival of the habit**. If they show up for 10 years and "only" become a decent hobbyist who loves music, you won't have wasted a dime.
