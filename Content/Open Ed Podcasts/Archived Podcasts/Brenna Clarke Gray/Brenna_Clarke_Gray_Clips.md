## CLIP 1: Teaching is Relational Work

**Story Arc:**
- **Setup** (15-20s): Define what good teaching is
- **Tension** (30-40s): Contrast relational vs. transactional learning
- **Resolution** (20-30s): The consequence of missing relationality

**Brenna Clarke Gray (05:26.574 - 05:55.515)**

"Teaching and learning is relational work. If you don't have a relationship with the person who is trying to teach you, and that doesn't have to mean anything more than a sense that they care about your progress through the discipline, your understanding of the material, I think it becomes that's when it's knowledge transfer. And you might as well watch a video lecture at that point because there's no relationality, there's no connection there. ~~I've always been really interested in how difficult it is to convince people that~~ Any kind of active learning strategy—whether it is project-based learning or using gamification—is about creating opportunities for students to make new connections in their brain with the material."

## CLIP 2: We Measure Compliance, Not Learning

**Story Arc:**
- **Setup** (15-20s): Identify the core problem in education
- **Tension** (40-50s): Show the specific ways we measure wrong + systemic constraints
- **Resolution** (15-25s): The bigger issue we ignore

**Brenna Clarke Gray (06:46.546 - 07:40.039)**

"Traditional methods in schools are often based on compliance. It's based on what can I measure and how well can you achieve this thing that demonstrates the measure. None of which is really learning. Measuring learning is so hard to do. It's really easy to measure compliance. How many times did you come to class? How many times did you raise your hand? How many exercises did you hand in? ~~And so I think we often end up in a system because of the constraints~~ We find ourselves looking for what we can measure rather than looking for where the learning is taking place. And we would all be better off if we acknowledged how hard it is to measure whether somebody is learning something or not."

---

**On-Screen Text Options:**

1. "We measure what's easy, not what matters"
2. "Compliance ≠ Learning"
3. "We count attendance instead of understanding"
4. "Measuring learning is actually really hard"
5. "Stop measuring butts in seats"
6. "What gets measured gets managed—but is it learning?"
7. "The problem with education: we're measuring the wrong things"

## CLIP 3: Boredom is How Kids Find Their Passions

**Story Arc:**
- **Setup** (15-20s): The surprising claim about boredom
- **Tension** (45-55s): Real-world example of child discovering lifelong interest through unstructured time
- **Resolution** (15-20s): The trade-off we're making

**Brenna Clarke Gray (30:57.528 - 32:35.426)**

"Boredom is incredibly important to learning. My eight-year-old loves mythology from anywhere in the world. Stacks and stacks of mythology books, listens to mythology podcasts all the time. ~~And that 100% came out of~~ That came out of boredom, particularly during the pandemic closure period. My kiddo was at the exact age, just starting to read on their own, and ~~had a lot of time to~~ figure stuff out. So many new interests emerged in that period that have become *lifelong* interests. *My kiddo still loves anything to do with mythology, anything to do with the ancient world. Just reading to me last night a book about ancient Egypt.* This year we added martial arts and flute, so there's suddenly much less time for boredom and much less time for just searching out random interesting facts. I'm seeing the benefits of commitment and discipline, but there's a trade-off—less time for mythology books and library trips."

---

**On-Screen Text Options:**

1. "Boredom is how kids discover their passions"
2. "Unstructured time matters"
3. "Let your kids be bored"
4. "Kids need space to explore their own interests"
5. "Mythology, ancient Egypt, random facts—all from boredom"
6. "Filling every moment with activity kills curiosity"
7. "The trade-off: structure vs. serendipity"
