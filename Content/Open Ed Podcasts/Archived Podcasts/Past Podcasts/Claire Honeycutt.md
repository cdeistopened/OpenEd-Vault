    - How to program computers to perform these calculations
    - Critical thinking skills to validate computer-generated results

The author presents a compelling case for teaching history without focusing on date memorization, using her 10-year-old daughter as an example. Her daughter demonstrates comprehensive understanding of historical relationships and causality across various civilizations and time periods, despite never having memorized specific dates.

Key aspects her daughter understands include:

- The chronological relationship between major civilizations (Greeks vs Romans)
- Complex historical transitions (Roman Empire's fall, Byzantine Empire)
- Cross-cultural connections (like the link between Maori people and the Trojan War)
- Architectural history (Hagia Sofia, Versailles)
- Global historical developments across multiple continents and cultures

thread about why she homeschools her children, from her perspective as a university professor:

1. Traditional Lecture Learning is Ineffective

- Promotes linear thinking
- Leads to students simply mimicking their teacher's thought patterns

1. Active Learning is Superior

- Involves projects, discussions, and experiments
- Increases both knowledge acquisition and retention
- Leads to deeper understanding

1. Mixed-Age Learning Environments are Beneficial

- Younger and older students learning together
- Older students benefit from teaching younger ones
- Promotes kindness and community-mindedness
- Develops caring attitudes and community values

1. Student-Directed Learning Builds Independence

- Gives students choice in what they study
- Increases intrinsic motivation
- Leads to deeper, more applied knowledge
- Students often choose more interesting subjects than teachers would plan
- Results in broader learning across more topics

## **The Case Against Grammar Worksheets (And What To Do Instead)**

Remember the joys of learning about the past perfect progressive tense or diagramming prepositional phrases? Yeahâ€¦ neither do we.

When **[Claire Honeycutt (@HippyMomPhD)](https://x.com/HippyMomPhD?utm_source=opened-daily.beehiiv.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=088-the-case-against-grammar-worksheets-and-what-to-do-instead)** tweeted that "most kids don't need grammar lessons, they need great books," she sparked quite the debate. While we're neutral on specific educational approaches, she raises an interesting point:

Kids are learning machines. They picked up their first language without a single worksheet on gerunds. So we may be overthinking this whole grammar thing.

Here's a simple framework for the natural grammar approach:

1. Surround them with great books
2. Save formal grammar for when they need it (like learning a second language)
3. Focus on the practical stuff (yes, knowing where commas go is useful)
