## **Your Computer Is a Vegas Casino**

The simplest way to describe it is that your computer is trying its best to simulate a Vegas casino. Casinos figured out how to design a light environment that makes people most susceptible to dopamine loops:

1. Block out all natural light.
2. Crank the brightness.
3. Make everything flicker.
4. Oversaturate the colors.

Basically, you out the nervous system on high alert, and suddenly people become maximally vulnerable to getting stuck in consumptive, stimming loops <eliminate some redunancy here>.

Your kid's Chromebook or iPad was designed‚Äîunwittingly or not‚Äîthe same way.

Anjan breaks down the 5 mechanisms at work with the precision of someone who's spent years studying the physics of displays:

1. **Blue light.** Blue light from screens affects your vagal tone, your nervous system, your breathing patterns, your blink rate. It disrupts your dopamine system, your melatonin production, your circadian rhythms, and all the metabolic processes that cascade from those systems. Your body thinks it's noon when it's midnight. Every screen session is a small jetlag.
2. **Flicker.** Thought invisible to your conscious perception, your nervous system detects tge LED backlight pulsing on and off, sometimes hundreds of times per second. Your brain registers this as subtle, chronic stress. Even "flicker-free" screens aren't actually flicker-free‚Äîthey just flicker faster than you can consciously detect.
3. **Brightness mismatch.** Your screen is always either brighter or dimmer than your environment, which forces constant adaptation. This creates what researchers call "accommodation stress"‚Äîyour pupils are perpetually adjusting, your eye muscles are perpetually straining. It's subtle, but chronic. The equivalent of having someone randomly turn a dimmer switch up and down all day while you're trying to focus.
4. **Oversaturated colors.** Modern displays can produce colors engineered to be more vivid, more intense, more attention-grabbing than anything in the natural world. This isn't by accident‚Äîit's by design. Oversaturated colors overstimulate your dopamine centers. Your brain gets a little hit of novelty with every scroll, every swipe, every new notification.
5. **Indoor captivity.** Lastly, backlit screens are nearly impossible to read in direct sunlight, which means you're trapped inside. The screen doesn't just occupy your attention‚Äîit dictates your physical location.

The combination of these five mechanisms leads to something call "screen apnea" (like sleep apnea, but for screens).

"Basically, because you have sympathetic overregulation and not enough parasympathetic, you actually breathe less when you're on a computer," Anjan explained. "You take less breaths per minute, and your tidal volume is less, meaning you breathe shallower. The combination of these two is you're dysregulated. You're a bit more anxious."

You're dysregulated before you even open TikTok.

Seen through this lens, your kid's post-iPad meltdown makes more sense. Their nervous system has been in subtle fight-or-flight for however long they've been on the screen, and when you take it away, they crash.

Adults aren‚Äôt immune either‚Äîwe just have better emotional regulation (most of the time), so it manifests as irritability, difficulty focusing, that vague sense of being fried after a long day of screen work.

## Finish Your Work and Go Outside

I have a mantra taped to my computer monitor that reads "Finish your work and go outside."

It's only when I actually emerge that my view expands‚Äîliterally. My eyes relax. My shoulders drop. I remember why I needed to go outside so badly.

Media theorist Marshall McLuhan called this "the hypertrophy of the eye." Modernity, McLuhuan, thought had led to a grotesque overdevelopment of one sense at the expense of all others. Ever since Gutenberg, life has centered more and more around the printed word. And in the past 50 years, that word has moved to the screen. McLuhan wasn't speaking metaphorically. Your eye muscles actually lock when you stare at closeup screens for hours. My own eyesight became ‚Äúprogressively myopic‚Äù (read: nearsighted) around sophomore year of high school and kept getting worse for the next decade.

The hours of Nintendo 64, iMovie projects, and being terminally online before anyone called it that. It was fun and novel until I realized it was making me depressed.

When one sense hypertrophies, the others atrophy. Hyperfocus on the visual means loss of proprioception (where your body is in space), peripheral vision, and awareness of your environment.

Have you ever wondered why your best ideas never come when you‚Äôre hunched over a laptop?

"Unstructured time is the fundamental substrate for creativity," Anjan told me. Your clearest thinking happens with a notepad under a tree, looking up occasionally, letting your eyes relax into the distance. Not trapped in the tunnel vision of a backlit screen.

## The Impossible Problem and the Naive Outsider Who Solved It

## The Bicycle, the Tool, and What You Pass On
