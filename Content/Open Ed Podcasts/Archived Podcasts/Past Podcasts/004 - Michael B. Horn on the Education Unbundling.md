## Blog: The Great Unbundling: Why Education is Ripe for Disruption

It‚Äôs 2024. You can customize everything from your coffee order to your car's playlist. So why are kids still stuck in a one-size-fits-all classroom?

Imagine if Amazon only sold one product, or if Netflix had just one show. Sounds ridiculous, but that's how we've been doing education for the past century.

Our recent podcast with Michael Horn started the same way most conversations about education have since Sir Ken Robinson's iconic 2006 TED talk: the education system has been breaking for a long time.

"My perception," Horn began, "is that one-size-fits-all was never the right way to educate kids or serve families."

Yet despite near universal agreement on the problem, the solution has been more elusive.

Horn, co-founder of the Clayton Christensen Institute and author of "From Reopen to Reinvent" and "Choosing College," argues that while change is coming, the current system isn't going to disappear overnight.

Many well-intentioned reformers have tried to fix education through top-down, system-wide changes. They propose sweeping policies, implement new standardized tests, or introduce technology into classrooms _en masse_. But time and again, these efforts fall short.

Why?

As Horn explains, "Public schools are part of a complex value network involving regulators, voters, parents, teachers, unions, and more."

This intricate web of stakeholders and interests makes the system resistant to change. Horn's mentor, Clayton Christensen (the "jobs to be done" guru), might say that schools fulfill a specific job for society ‚Äì¬†equal parts education and babysitting.

"Transforming an existing system is so hard," Horn notes, "because new ideas that don't fit into the established processes and priorities are often rejected."

So, dramatic changes are met with resistance, and the fundamental structure of education remains unchanged.

## The Great Unbundling

Instead of trying to overhaul the entire system at once, Horn argues for what he calls an ‚Äúunbundling" of education. This approach recognizes that meaningful change often comes from outside the existing system, in a more piecemeal fashion.

Education Savings Accounts (ESAs) are one of the main drivers of this unbundling. These programs, now available in at least 14 states, grant families a scholarship for a variety of educational options.

"My guess is around 10 million students or so are actively taking advantage of choice options," Horn estimates.

However, most families start by spending ESA dollars on traditional private schools, so the true unbundling happens gradually.

As Horn explains, "The longer students remain in the program, the share of ESA funds devoted to private school tuition decreases while expenditure shares increase for curriculum, instruction, tutoring, and specialized services." In other words, families must _learn_ to customize their children's education, a process that typically unfolds gradually over time.

While data from most ESA programs shows that about 90% of funds still go to a single provider‚Äîtypically private schools‚ÄîUtah is bucking this trend. The forecast for Utah suggests that only 50% of ESA funds will go to single providers, indicating a much higher rate of customization and unbundling.

Matt Bowman, founder of OpenEd, attributes this difference to his organization's long-standing presence in the state. As he puts it, "My Tech High‚ÄîOpenEd now‚Äîhas been around for 15 years, showing that there's a way to '√† la carte' your child's education."

By demonstrating the viability and benefits of personalized, flexible education options for over a decade, OpenEd has primed Utah families to utilize ESAs from the start.

## Not Your Grandfather‚Äôs ‚ÄúSchool Choice‚Äù

The distinction between ESAs and traditional vouchers is crucial here. While vouchers typically offer a substitute one-size-fits-all solution, ESAs provide the flexibility for true customization. However, as Horn points out, this customizability is only valuable if there's a vibrant marketplace of options for families to choose from.

"We need to focus on creating entirely new systems," Horn argues, "rather than trying to transform the existing ones." This means building a robust ecosystem of educational providers, resources, and models that can cater to diverse needs without demanding the full-time parent-as-teacher presence often associated with traditional homeschooling.

Yet, it's important to acknowledge that for many families, the current system is working well enough. The frictions involved in customizing education‚Äîresearching options, coordinating schedules, managing multiple providers‚Äîcan make it unattractive to those who are basically satisfied with traditional schools. As Horn notes, "We shouldn't expect an unbundling of school to happen en masse or right away."

The path forward, then, is not about forcing change, but about creating opportunities for those who seek them. It's about building sustainable, innovative models like OpenEd that can serve as examples and catalysts. As more families see these alternatives in action, the "Great Unbundling" of education will continue to unfold‚Äînot as a sudden revolution, but as a gradual evolution towards a more personalized, flexible future of learning.

> Foremost among them is this: Despite all the fancy models and white papers around what are all the levers to pull in order to transform a system, system transformation almost never happens by changing the fundamental tenets of the system itself.     Key Insights:
