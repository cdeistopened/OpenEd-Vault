# **The Reading Crisis No One's Talking About: 5 Myths That Keep Children Struggling**

Around 66% of children in the U.S. are behind on reading. 70% of those never catch up. About half of American adults read below a sixth-grade level.

Let's examine the five most harmful myths about reading struggles, and what Tony Mickelsen and the evidence tell us instead.

## **Myth #1: "Some kids just aren't natural readers."**

This common refrain suggests that reading ability is primarily innateâ€”some children are "born readers" while others simply aren't wired that way. This belief often leads to lowered expectations and premature surrender when challenges arise.

**The truth:**Â Reading is a skill, not an inherent talent. While children learn at different paces, virtually all neurotypical children can become proficient readers with appropriate instruction.

During the podcast, Mickelsen emphasized that Savvy Learning's approach is effective for all types of learners: "No matter whether it's a struggling reader, or it's a homeschool student who, you know, mom is busy with five other kids and can't quite be as consistent as she would like on the reading instruction, or, you know, it's a child who's actually accelerated and needs to be challenged in a way that a public school classroom cannot challenge them."

The key insight is that reading challenges typically stem from inadequate or mismatched instruction, not from some immutable limitation in the child. When we believe a child "just isn't a reader," we may unintentionally communicate lower expectations, provide fewer reading opportunities, or give up on interventions too quickly.

## **Myth #2: "More time in one session equals more learning."**

The conventional tutoring model typically involves hour-long sessions once or twice a week. This approach persists largely because of tradition and logistical convenience for adults, not because it's optimal for children's learning.

**The truth:**Â Shorter, more frequent sessions work dramatically better than longer, less frequent onesâ€”especially for struggling readers.

Mickelsen was unequivocal on this point: "Our format for high-dose tutoring is four days a week, Monday through Thursday, 25 minutes per day. Well, for children who have short attention spans, this makes a lot more sense because what child is going to have fun while learning to read or do math for 60 minutes? It's really tough. That's tough for me as an adult, let alone for the little kids."

This isn't just theoretical. Mickelsen cited research showing that "high-dose tutoring is 20 times more effective than standard tutoring." The key difference? Breaking down that hour or two of weekly tutoring into shorter, more frequent sessions.

The high-dosage approach ensures that every minute is productive by keeping sessions brief enough to maintain engagement throughout. The more frequent schedule also creates momentum and minimizes forgetting between sessions.

Mickelsen explains the results they observe: "The kids at the end of a session with Savvy, they are hyped. They are having so much fun that they want to come back for the next session."

## **Myth #3: "Reading struggles mean lower intelligence."**

Perhaps the most harmful myth is the implicit (and sometimes explicit) association between reading difficulties and overall intelligence. This false correlation causes unnecessary shame and can permanently damage a child's academic self-concept.

**The truth:**Â Reading challenges have no correlation with overall intelligence or potential.

When children struggle with this fundamental skill, they often feel inherently deficient in ways that extend far beyond reading itself. What makes this particularly insidious is how reading difficulties tend to mask themselves as other problems.

What presents as behavioral issues or social difficulties often has reading struggles as the root cause. Mickelsen has heard this story repeatedly from parents who find Savvy Learning: "The improvement we've made on reading has improved my relationship with my daughter so much. Because now I'm not nagging her so much about the whole reading thing."

What looked like a behavioral issue or a relationship problem was actually a reading issue all along.

Around fourth grade, education shifts from "learning to read" to "reading to learn." Students who haven't mastered basic literacy by this transition point suddenly find themselves unable to access the curriculum across all subjects.

The crucial insight is that reading difficulties don't indicate lower intelligenceâ€”they simply create barriers to demonstrating and developing intelligence through traditional educational pathways.

## **Myth #4: "Tutoring is only for kids who are way behind."**

Many parents view tutoring as a last resortâ€”something to consider only when a child is significantly behind grade level or failing in school.

**The truth:**Â Reading support benefits children at all levels, from those who are struggling to those who are already on track.

Mickelsen explained that Savvy's high-dosage tutoring works for various situations, not just for children who are significantly behind: "We just meet the child where they're at on their reading or math journey. We assess them, we have years worth of content, and we plug them into the reading or math level where they're at. And from there, the high dosage tutoring thing propels them forward."

The key is providing the right level of support and challenge for each individual child. For students who are behind, appropriate tutoring can help them catch up. For students at grade level, it can help them develop deeper comprehension and greater reading fluency. For advanced readers, it can provide the appropriate level of challenge that might be missing in a regular classroom.

## **Myth #5: "My child will catch up naturally over time."**

Perhaps the most dangerous myth is the belief that reading difficulties will resolve themselves with time and normal development. This "wait and see" approach remains surprisingly common advice, even from some educators.

**The truth:**Â Without appropriate intervention, reading gaps typically widen rather than narrow over time.

The statistics Mickelsen shared are unequivocalâ€”most children who fall behind in reading never catch up without specific intervention: "66% of children in the US are behind on reading, that 70% of those never catch up, and that about half of adults in the US read below a sixth grade level."

What begins as a small gap in early elementary school can grow into a chasm by middle school as the curriculum shifts from "learning to read" to "reading to learn."

This is why early intervention is so crucial. While it's never too late to improve reading skills, addressing difficulties in the early years is significantly more efficient and effective than remediation in later years.

For decades, evidence-based reading interventions remained largely inaccessible to individual families. High-dosage tutoring existed primarily within institutional settingsâ€”if you were lucky enough to attend a school that offered it.

By making high-dosage tutoring directly available to families, Savvy Learning is democratizing access to what actually works. No waiting for school districts to adopt new programs. No navigating bureaucratic approval processes. Just immediate access to evidence-based intervention.

## **The Parental Involvement Factor**

Another critical advantage of direct-to-parent tutoring is the involvement it facilitates. When tutoring happens through schools, parents often remain largely uninformed about the process, content, and progress.

"When you have high-dose tutoring that is happening through school districts, the parents are not completely left out of the loop, [but] largely left out of the loop because it's happening at school," Mickelsen noted.

The reading crisis in America is real, severe, and far-reaching. Two-thirds of our children struggle with reading, and most never catch up without targeted intervention.
