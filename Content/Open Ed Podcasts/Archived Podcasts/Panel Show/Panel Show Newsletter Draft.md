# The frictionless parenting trap

Michelle Rhee spent years reforming schools and now she's got something to say to parents: you're doing it wrong. Not the helicopter parenting thing everyone talks about‚Äîsomething more subtle and way more damaging. "I think that for many women of my generation who are working moms, we had that sort of like working mom guilt," she says. "And so when we did spend time with our kids, we wanted to make everything so like frictionless." Of course we did. We felt bad about missing bedtime stories, so we overcompensated by removing every possible obstacle from their lives. But here's the thing Rhee discovered: "When we do that as parents, we do a disservice to our kids." The research backs this up‚Äîkids who have to navigate hard things become more successful adults. Which means our guilt-driven parenting is accidentally creating the exact opposite of what we want: confident, capable humans.

# Kids are making millions in careers that don't exist in college

Here's a story that'll make you question everything: Michelle Rhee moves into this fancy LA apartment building, super expensive place, and realizes she and her husband are literally the oldest people there. Times two. So naturally she starts asking: what do all these kids do for money? The answer? "They're in cannabis, they're in crypto, they're gamers, they are content creators." None of these existed when we were planning our college majors, and exactly zero of them require a degree. Isaac Morehouse nails it: "Those are things that they couldn't have even tried because 10 years ago they didn't exist." Meanwhile, we're still obsessing over getting kids into "good colleges" for careers that might not exist by the time they graduate. The parents of these crypto-rich 22-year-olds are probably freaking out right now because their kids didn't follow the script. But maybe the script is the problem.

# Getting the reps in builds real confidence

Isaac Morehouse learned this the hard way with his oldest kid: we're accidentally crushing our children's natural confidence. Watch any toddler‚Äîthey see mom cutting vegetables, they want to try. They see dad mowing the lawn, they grab for the handle. And what do we do? "I was too quick to be like, no, you can't do this. I'm trying to get something done." Sound familiar? Michelle Rhee calls it "getting the reps in"‚Äîthe same way athletes build muscle memory through repetition. "If you watch something enough times or hear something enough times, you sort of gain confidence. And then young people are gonna be like, okay, I've seen it. I have some pattern recognition now. Like, I can do this." The solution isn't complicated: let them try, let them fail, let them figure it out. Yes, they'll do it worse than you. Yes, it'll take longer. But that's how humans learn to be human.
