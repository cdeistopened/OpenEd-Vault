## **Trend: Parents Are "Rewilding" Their Kids**

There's a trend emerging among parents who are rejecting the hyper-scheduled, resume-building childhood. They're starting to "rewild" their kids. And it has nothing to do with nature.

This is about cultivating an _intellectual_ wilderness. As Michael Gibson puts it, itâ€™s about creating an environment where children can develop real capability, not just credentials. It's a deliberate move away from the structured paths that produce what he calls a "brittle dependence" on institutions.

In practice, this means giving kids unsolved problems, letting them get bored, encouraging them to build things without instructions, and celebrating the brilliant question over the correct answer. It's about trusting that the project, the experiment, the thing they build for themselves, is where the deepest learning happens.

## **Tool: The Contrarian Fund That Bets on Cool Ideas**

Gibson argues that for young builders, the startup is the "real school." So his fund, 1517, acts accordingly. With a better track record of picking unicorn founders than Y-Combinator, they operate on a radical principle of permissionless innovation.

While most VCs want a 20-page deck and a warm intro, 1517 offers grants of $1,000 to anyone, anywhere, with a cool idea. You don't need to be in Silicon Valley. You don't need a college degree. You just need to be working on something interesting.

It's a tool designed to find the next George Dantzig or Dylan Field, and you can apply today.
