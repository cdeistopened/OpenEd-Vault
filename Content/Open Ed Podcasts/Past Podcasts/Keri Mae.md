### How many hours is your kid wasting

Your child spends 7+ hours daily in school. Most educators know that many of those hours are wasted with unnecessary worksheets, waiting for others, and checking boxes.

More time doesn‚Äôt mean more learning.

Here‚Äôs how our OpenEd students spend their time instead:

- Learning advanced math at his pace
- Studying a new language
- Training with a personal trainer
- Mastering real-world physics through building robots
- Building a real business and learning life lessons along the way

The traditional system works for some. But why accept lost time if there's a better way for your child?

  

### What Keri noticed:

A kindergarten teacher noticed vibrant, curious kids entering school full of life. By first grade? "I hate learning."

  
This wasn't just one classroom. After teaching hundreds of students, she saw the pattern repeat. Kids weren't struggling with learning - they were struggling with how we teach.

  
Her own autistic son faced similar challenges. The school's solution? A bandaid that just created new problems.

  
Then they found a different approach. The "behavior issues" vanished because the environment changed to fit the child.

  

### I‚Äôm a no-homework parent

‚ÄúI'm a strict no-homework parent."

  
Shocking words from a former public school teacher. But her reasoning is compelling: after studying early childhood development, she saw the disconnect. We know kids learn best through movement, emotional engagement, and hands-on experience.

  
Yet we send them home with worksheets.

  
Now her evenings look different.

  

Her kids cook dinner alongside her, learning measurement and chemistry. They climb rocks, building confidence and physical literacy. They practice real skills instead of repeating paper exercises.

  

School can end at school. Let evenings be for living.

  

### Zoom experience

"I used to be a stay-at-home mom who thought personalized education was impossible with a full-time job."

  
Now she's a single working parent doing the impossible.

  
Her turning point? A pandemic Zoom class where her advanced learner wrote: "You are all stupid." While others practiced basic letters, he was writing full sentences with perfect punctuation.  

That's when she realized: boredom isn't a behavior problem. It's a design flaw.  

Education should expand to meet excellence, not constrain it to keep pace.

  

### Teach them to become more capable ‚úÖ

Children are more capable than we allow them to be.

  

We shelter them from dangerous. But which one‚Äôs more dangerous: teaching them slowly & cautiously or leaving them unprepared for real life.

  
For millions of years, children learned by doing dangerous things carefully. Now we've traded competence for comfort.  

The result? Teenagers who feel unsafe in a world they've never learned to navigate.

  
Real confidence comes from real capability.

  

### Rethink education

In real life, we don't use calculus daily. We don't remember all five oceans. We specialize in what we love and hire experts for the rest.

  
Yet we force children to master everything equally.  

Stop preparing children for a world that no longer exists.

  

### No homework

Young minds spend all day at school, absorbing new information, navigating social situations, and staying focused. They need their evenings for:

- Physical movement
- Family connection
- Unstructured play
- Rest and recovery
- Life skills development

Families who don‚Äôt do homework are surprised.

The surprise? Their grades didn't drop. Their learning didn't suffer. If anything, they're more engaged at school because they're well-rested and emotionally fulfilled.

# OpenEd Podcast: Personalizing Education as a Single Parent

Guest: Keri Mae  
Host: Ela Richmond

## Introduction [00:01]

As a former public school educator with a degree in early childhood development, Keri Mae brings unique insight to personalizing education for her three boys (ages 6, 10, and 12) as a single parent. Her educational journey began in traditional public school, where her oldest son, being neurotypical and a high achiever, adapted well to the conventional system. However, the path shifted when her middle son, who is autistic, began experiencing significant challenges, particularly with sensory processing issues that led to night terrors triggered by school bells.

## Finding Alternative Paths [03:34]

The search for a better fit led Keri Mae to a Montessori charter school, where many of their previous challenges naturally dissolved. The school's gentle approach eliminated harsh transitions, allowed children to wear special slippers or go barefoot, and created a more nurturing environment. As a public charter school, it provided the additional benefit of being financially accessible for a single-parent family.

## The Pandemic Pivot [05:08]

The COVID-19 pandemic became an unexpected catalyst for change. When schools became inconsistent, Keri Mae was forced to explore homeschooling while working full-time. This experience revealed that alternative education could be viable even with her work commitments. She established age 12 as a threshold for homeschooling, recognizing that older children could work more independently while she managed her professional responsibilities.

## A Day in the Life [10:31]

Her 12-year-old's current education combines structured learning with flexibility. Each day includes online math programs, language studies, and hybrid language arts assignments that Keri Mae grades herself. Physical education takes the form of rock climbing, which the family does either early morning or after school. The schedule also includes sessions at 4D Learning Academy for science and technology, and regular personal training.

## Working Parent Strategy [21:54]

Managing education while working full-time requires careful balance. Keri Mae maintains accountability through weekly progress reports from online programs and clear consequences for off-task behavior. Working remotely allows her to supervise while completing her own work, often taking her laptop to various educational settings. Rather than directing every moment, she creates environments that encourage learning and independence.

## School Engagement [23:43]

Staying involved with her children's education takes creative scheduling. Keri Mae uses lunch breaks or requests time off for special events to volunteer at the Montessori school. She maintains open communication with teachers through early engagement and regular check-ins. Each day ends with "Rosebud and Thorn" conversations, where family members share their daily highlights and challenges.

## The Homework Philosophy [26:06]

Based on educational research, Keri Mae maintains a strict no-homework policy for elementary grades. This decision protects family time and allows for crucial life skill development. Evening hours are dedicated to reading together, collaborative cooking, and physical activity. When teachers express concerns about preparation for higher grades, she advocates for alternative approaches that don't require traditional homework.

## Learning Through Experience [39:00]

Keri Mae emphasizes the importance of hands-on learning through her family's involvement in primitive skills training. This approach demonstrates how understanding principles proves more valuable than memorizing facts. She encourages age-appropriate risk-taking and practical skills development, believing children are more capable than society often assumes.

## Success Through Trust [43:31]

One powerful example of this philosophy in action was her 12-year-old forging his own knife with a professional blacksmith. This experience not only taught practical skills but built tremendous confidence. Keri Mae believes in introducing children to real tools and responsibilities early, teaching proper safety and building competence through trust and guidance.

## Looking Forward [47:47]

As her children grow older, Keri Mae anticipates new challenges. Her middle son approaches the homeschooling threshold, requiring a completely different educational approach than his older brother. She recognizes the increasing importance of peer relationships in the teenage years and plans to adapt their educational strategy accordingly.

## The Heart of Open Education [49:04]

Keri Mae's story demonstrates that open education isn't about choosing between traditional school or homeschool exclusively. Instead, it's about finding the right combination of educational opportunities that work for each child and family situation. As a single working parent, she's proven that personalized education remains possible even within constraints, provided one remains flexible and attentive to each child's unique needs and developmental stage.
