## Types of Homeschool Art Curriculum

Before you buy a box of expensive pastels, you need to know what *kind* of art education you are aiming for. Most curricula fall into one of three philosophical buckets.

### 1. Process Art vs. Product Art
This is the "great dichotomy" of early art education.
*   **Process Art:** Values the journey. It is open-ended explorationâ€”think finger painting, clay squishing, or collage with no rules. The goal is sensory experience and creative confidence. Itâ€™s about *doing*, not *making*.
*   **Product Art:** Values the outcome. It focuses on following steps to create a specific result (e.g., "Draw a bird like this"). This builds discipline and specific techniques.
*   **The Sweet Spot:** The best homeschool art programs blend these. They might start with a specific technique (product) but then encourage the child to apply it freely (process).

### 2. The Charlotte Mason Approach (Picture Study)
If you want to raise a child who appreciates beauty, this is your model. Charlotte Mason emphasized the "Habit of Attention." Instead of drilling dates and names, you study *one* artist for weeks.
*   **Observation:** The child looks at a painting silently for 3-5 minutes.
*   **Narration:** The painting is hidden, and the child describes it in detail from memory.
*   This trains the eye to see detail and nuanceâ€”a skill that translates to science, writing, and every other subject.

### 3. Unit Studies (The Integrated Approach)
Here, art is not a silo; it is the visual language of whatever else you are studying.
*   **History Integration:** If you are studying the Civil War, your art project is sewing period costumes or drawing 1860s architecture.
*   **STEAM:** If you are studying anatomy, you are doing technical drawings of skeletal structures.
*   This is the most efficient way to teach art, as it "double dips" your study time.

## Logistics: Keeping the Sanity

You cannot teach art if you are terrified of the mess.

1.  **The Art Cart:** Put everything on a rolling cart. Roll it to the table for "Art Time," roll it into a closet when done.
2.  **The Digital Archive:** You do not need to keep every doodle. Photograph their work, upload it to a digital folder, and recycle the physical copy (keep only the absolute bests).
3.  **Grading:** For high schoolers, grade on *objective* criteria, not "talent." Did they follow the instructions? Did they complete the assignment? Did they show effort?
