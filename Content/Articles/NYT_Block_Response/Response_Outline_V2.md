# Response Outline V2: What the NYT Gets Wrong About Unschooling

**Working Title:** What the New York Times Gets Wrong About Unschooling
**Length:** ~800-1000 words
**Tone:** Measured, empathetic, but clear

---

## The Angle

Three weeks ago, the New York Times published "America's Children Are Unwell. Are Schools Part of the Problem?" - an honest look at how schools may be causing the mental health crisis.

This weekend, they published a guest essay calling for mandatory state oversight of homeschoolers.

The Times is arguing with itself. And both pieces cite Peter Gray.

---

## Structure

### 1. The Self-Refuting Times (~150 words)

Open with the juxtaposition:
- November 24: Schools may be the problem. 1 in 4 boys with ADHD. Anxiety onset at age 6. "We need to fix the kids" instead of fixing schools.
- December 14: Homeschooling needs more oversight. Register with the state. Mandatory assessments.

The Nirvana Fallacy: Block compares homeschooling to an imagined school system where mandated reporters catch every abused child. But the Times' own reporting shows the actual school system may be *causing* harm.

### 2. What Block Describes Isn't Unschooling (~200 words)

Block invokes John Holt, but what he describes is the opposite of Holt's vision.

**Holt's unschooling:** Child-led learning that *expands* a child's world - community, mentors, real-world engagement, curiosity.

**Block's experience:** A struggling mother who *contracted* her son's world - isolation, enmeshment, using him to fill an emotional void.

Quote Holt directly: "Children learn from anything and everything they see. They learn wherever they are, not just in special learning places."

Block wasn't unschooled. He was isolated by a parent in crisis. That's a tragedy - but it's not an indictment of unschooling.

### 3. The Abuse Question (~200 words)

We take child abuse seriously. So does the homeschooling community.

But Block's argument assumes:
- Schools reliably catch abuse (they often don't)
- Homeschooling increases abuse risk (no evidence)
- State oversight would have saved him (unclear)

The honest comparison: abuse happens in both settings. The question is whether mandatory oversight of millions of families is justified by rare cases - or whether it creates new problems (chilling effect on legitimate homeschoolers, state overreach, bureaucratic burden).

We don't have to resolve this debate to note that Block's framing is one-sided.

### 4. The Outcomes Question (~150 words)

Block is a published novelist writing for the New York Times.

This isn't to minimize his suffering. But it does complicate the "educational neglect" framing. His unstructured years produced someone who can write, think, and engage with ideas at the highest level.

Research on unschooling outcomes (Gray/Riley study): 83% of grown unschoolers said the approach gave them an advantage in higher education.

### 5. What Unschooling Actually Looks Like (~200 words)

Link to our deep dive. Brief examples:
- North Star Teens (Ken Danford)
- Self-directed learning centers
- Families following children's interests
- The opposite of isolation

Close with: The debate about homeschool regulation is legitimate. But it should be based on what homeschooling and unschooling actually are - not on one man's painful experience with a troubled parent.

## What We're NOT Saying

- We're not dismissing Block's pain
- We're not saying abuse doesn't happen in homeschool settings
- We're not taking a strong stance against all oversight
- We're not attacking Block personally

## What We ARE Saying

- What Block experienced wasn't unschooling as Holt defined it
- The Times is self-refuting (schools cause harm / homeschools need oversight)
- The Nirvana Fallacy distorts this debate
- Unschooling, properly understood, expands children's worlds
