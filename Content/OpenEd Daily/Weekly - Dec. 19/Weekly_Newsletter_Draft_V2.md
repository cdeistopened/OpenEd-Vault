DEEP DIVE: What the Times Missed About Unschooling

Last Sunday, the New York Times just published a guest essay titled, "The Home-Schooled Kids Are Not All Right" - a pretty sweeping claim based on the author's (admittedly awful) experience. Stefan Merrill Block describes a childhood of isolation, a struggling mother, and unmet childhood needs. Understandably, he doesn't want other kids to go through that.

His pain is real. And he makes points worth hearing: isolation can harm children, some kids fall through the cracks, and adults outside the home sometimes see what parents miss.

But then he calls for mandatory registration, assessments, and check-ins for all homeschoolers. This argument rests on a comparison between his experience and a system that would have caught his pain. Teachers who would have noticed. Counselors who would have helped.

That school system doesn't exist.

In his response, Isaac Morehouse points out that this is a good example of the Nirvana Fallacy: comparing a real situation to an imagined ideal. It's worth noting that three weeks before this essay, the same New York Times published another article: "America's Children Are Unwell. Are Schools Part of the Problem?" Their own data showed 1 in 4 teenage boys now diagnosed with ADHD, anxiety spiking as early as age 6.

Peter Gray, a research professor at Boston College, has tracked this for years: every fall when school starts, teen mental health tanks. ER visits for anxiety spike. Psychiatric hospitalizations rise. Summer comes and it improves. When researchers asked teenagers what stresses them out, 83% cited school. Nothing else came close.

As Peter Gray puts it: "Instead of saying, 'We need to fix the schools,' the message is, 'We need to fix the kids.'"

Dr. Claire Honeycutt responded to the NY Times article saying,Â "The biggest problem in education today is the assumption that there is one perfect way to learn that suits all children everywhere under all conditions. That's never been true. No single school - or education model will fit all kids. The biggest gift we can give future generations is freedom. Freedom to not be confined to a single, failing system. The New York Times doesn't know my children or my neighbor's children. How can they possibly know what's best for my kids? They can't. We were a far better educated country before we tried to strip out all individuation and uniqueness out of our citizenry. Our best teachers leave because they are no longer allowed to fit the way they teach to their classrooms. If you want people to return to public school, make it something worth returning to. Until then, let the parents who are upending their entire lives to be with their children do their work in peace.
They are not the problem."

Our message to parents is simpler: you don't have to be a perfect parent to do better than the status quo if school is failing your kid.

If rigid one-size-fits-all schooling represents one end of the spectrum, unschooling represents the other. Like any approach, it can be done well or poorly. But with a safe, supportive environment, you might be surprised how little coercion and formal curriculum is required to let your kid thrive.

So what does unschooling look like when it's done right?

Amanda Schenkenberger, an OpenEd mom and homeschool coach recently interviewed on Fox News, put it simply: "Unschooling is pursuing a level of learning and following my kids' lead to what they're curious about and diving deeply into the subject as long as their curiosity is alive about it."

As OpenEd students, her boys take Oregon's required standardized tests and score in the top 10 percent nationally. But test scores aren't really the point. The real argument for unschooling is that kids who drive their own learning actually want to learn - and that changes everything.

PODCAST HIGHLIGHT: She Did Everything Right - Her Daughter Still Couldn't Read

Janssen Bradshaw (@everydayreading) had every advantage. Former librarian. English major. She read aloud to her kids daily and had thousands of books in her home.

Her first daughter tested at a 12th grade reading level in kindergarten.

Her second daughter was barely at grade level in first grade - and drifting lower.

"It was not like she was not smart," Janssen told Ela, "But learning to read is just really hard for some kids."

What followed was a transformation. Janssen discovered what researcher Benjamin Bloom called the "Two Sigma Problem" - students who receive one-on-one tutoring outperform 98% of students taught in regular classrooms. The solution wasn't more hours or harder curriculum. It was 25-minute sessions, four times a week, meeting her daughter exactly where she was.

By fifth grade, her daughter was reading at an 11th-grade level.

When Janssen asked her daughter if she was embarrassed about needing extra help, she said: "Why would I be embarrassed that something was hard for me? And then I worked really hard and now I'm awesome at it."

That approach became Savvy Learning - an intensive "high dosage" tutoring program (delivered in small doses) now available in the OpenEd Marketplace.

TRENDS WE'RE FOLLOWING

The Times Does a 180 - Three weeks before publishing their homeschool piece, the New York Times Magazine ran "America's Children Are Unwell. Are Schools Part of the Problem?" One in four seventeen-year-old boys now diagnosed with ADHD. Autism diagnoses went from 1 in 2,500 in the 1980s to 1 in 31 today. Anxiety diagnoses in 32% of adolescents - median onset age of six. Peter Gray, quoted in the piece: "Instead of saying, 'We need to fix the schools,' the message is, 'We need to fix the kids.'" (NYT Magazine)

Readers Are Now the Minority - Essayist Paul Graham shares data: "Readers used to outnumber non-readers 2 to 1. Now non-readers outnumber readers 3 to 1." That's a complete inversion. Hard to imagine a shift that big not showing up everywhere. (Paul Graham)

Parents Want Skills, Not Just Scores - EdChoice polling finds 85% of parents rate "durable skills" - communication, critical thinking, teamwork, adaptability - as extremely or very important. 45% say a school's emphasis on these makes a "significant" impact on enrollment. Meanwhile, 73% of school parents now support Education Savings Accounts. (EdChoice)

Teachers and Students See Different Realities - 92% of students say engaging lessons make school more enjoyable, but only 63% say they feel "highly engaged." There's a 20-point gap between what students report and what teachers perceive. The report's conclusion: engagement isn't optional - it's a precondition for learning. (The 74)

The 8-Hour Day Is Made Up - We talk a lot about how the six-hour school day is an artifact of the factory model. But what about the eight-hour workday? Organizational psychologist Adam Grant: "The 8-hour work day was not ordained from above for all time. It was dreamed up by humans. We can be as productive and creative in 6 focused hours as in 8 unfocused hours." Anyone who's watched their kid finish "a full day of school" in two hours at home already knows this. (Adam Grant)

Writey the Whiteboard - Peel-and-stick whiteboards. Mark Cuban-backed. $30 for a 2x3 sheet. No drilling, repositionable. Math problems, spelling words, mapping your teenager's life decisions - a whiteboard handles all of it. (Writey)

The Feynman Technique - Richard Feynman had a simple method for learning anything: try to explain it to a child. If you can't, you don't understand it well enough. Go back and study the parts you stumbled on. Then try again. Four steps: choose a topic, explain it simply, identify gaps, revise. Works for parents learning alongside their kids and for kids tackling something new. (Educated and Free)

History Odyssey Timeline - Four timeline posters, 5 feet long each, where students enter historical events across 7 categories - Men and Women, Wars and Conflicts, Treaties and Agreements, and more. Visual learners love having history literally unfold in front of them. Can hang on the wall or fold into a binder. (Pandia Press)

From Hillsdale to Homeschooling - Practical advice from three Hillsdale alumnae with 30 combined years of homeschooling. Build community through co-ops. Let your calendar match your family - not the public school's. And learn to tell the difference between "can't" and "won't" when your kid says something's too hard. (Hillsdale K-12)

Navigating Holidays with Intense Kids - Colleen Kessler's guide for families with neurodivergent kids. Bring favorite snacks. Have an exit strategy. Let go of other people's expectations. "You didn't cause this. Their intensity isn't your failure." (Raising Lifelong Learners)

11 Things to Hear This Winter - Dallas Lore Sharp's nature study list from the early 1900s, still worth reading today. The three great silences of winter. The voice of the north wind through pine trees. The tinkle of water under thin ice. Chickadees in empty woods. Simple goals that turn noticing into a habit. (A Humble Place)

Free Kindergarten Resources - A Reddit thread with community-sourced recommendations for budget-conscious families. Real parents sharing what actually worked. (r/homeschool)
