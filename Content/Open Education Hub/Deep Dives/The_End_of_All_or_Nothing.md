Isaac Morehouse once described his family's educational journey this way: "Over the years, we had them in homeschool tutorials and co-ops, various at-home and online curricula, a twice-a-week microschool, a part-time Waldorf school, and every variation in between. Our oldest wanted to go to a public high school one year, so we let him. He decided not to go back the next year and instead to work full time, so we let him."

Now imagine someone at a dinner party asks Isaac the standard question: "So, what kind of education do your kids do?"

What's the one-word answer? Homeschool? No, there was public high school in there. And Waldorf. Public school? No, mostly home-based. Waldorf? Only part-time. Unschooling? There were structured curricula too.

There isn't a word for what Isaac's family did. And that's not because they were confused or uncommitted or doing it wrong. It's because we're trying to compress something multidimensional into a single-choice question.

The compression is the problem.

We've been asking the wrong question for 200 years. "Which institution?" "Which philosophy?" "Which method?" As if education is a multiple-choice test where you pick A, B, C, or D and live with everything that comes with that letter.

But education isn't a single choice. It's a design problem. And most familiesâ€”especially families with multiple kids, or kids with special needs, or kids who don't fit neatly into institutional boxesâ€”are already designing. They're already mixing and matching. They're already pulling from multiple sources.

They just don't have language for it. Or infrastructure supporting it. Or permission to admit it without sounding like they're making excuses.

Matt Bowman, who co-founded OpenEd and wrote the book *Open Education*, describes it this way: "We raised our children in the same home with the same routines and same expectations... yet it was clear that each child had their own unique set of needs and wants. If two parents raising five children in the same way resulted in very different pathways for each child, how could a standardized public education system offer a personalized learning journey for millions of children?"

Spoiler alert: It can't. Not alone, anyway.

But here's where it gets interesting: The same logic applies to *any* single approach. Traditional public school can't serve every child. Neither can private school. Neither can any single homeschool philosophy, no matter how brilliant or time-tested.

No single system can perfectly serve every child, or even the same child at every stage of their development. This isn't a controversial claim. Every parent knows it's true. We see it with our own eyes. Our kids are different from each other. They're different year to year. Sometimes they need structure. Sometimes they need space. Sometimes they thrive in groups. Sometimes they need to be alone.

Yet we keep pretending the answer is to find the One Right Institution or the One Right Method and stick with it. We compress reality into a single choice, then beat ourselves up when it doesn't quite fit.

Here's the math that makes this compression even more absurd: Your child spends roughly 1,260 hours per year in school. That's 35% of their waking time. The other 65%â€”2,390 hoursâ€”happens under your supervision, whether you planned for it or not.

Yet we treat that 35% like it's the whole game. Changing course feels like failure. Like we got it wrong. Like we're failing our kids by not figuring out how to make it work.

But what if the failure isn't yours? What if the failure is the compression itself? What if the failure is asking a multidimensional design question and only accepting single-choice answers?

Three barriers remained: **Knowledge. Resources. Community.**
