# Charlotte Mason Article - Source Material

## Core Message & Strategic Framing

**Main Thesis:** Charlotte Mason is a flexible, adaptable philosophy - not a rigid system. It's a set of principles you can apply however works for your family.

**OpenEd Constraint:** Cannot reimburse religious curricula (most CM curricula are Christian-based)

**Subtext:** Stop thinking in terms of what you can buy. Start thinking in terms of the general approach. CM principles work with any content - you don't need a "Charlotte Mason curriculum."

**Accessibility Angle:** Charlotte Mason's original writing can feel intimidating - Victorian prose, references to governesses and nurseries. It can seem like an approach designed for wealthy British families with household staff. Leah Boden's "Modern Miss Mason" exists specifically to make CM accessible and down-to-earth. We want to do the same - and OpenEd is there to help with curriculum and resources.

## Section-by-Section Substance

### Section 1: Understanding Charlotte Mason

**Who was she:**
- 19th-century British educator
- Founded a teacher training college and correspondence school
- Wrote a 6-volume series on education philosophy

**Core beliefs:**
- "Children are born persons" - whole people worthy of respect
- Education is the "science of relations" - connecting children to ideas, nature, art, history
- Learning happens through living books (real literature, not textbooks), short lessons, and direct experience

**Key principles:**
- Living books over textbooks
- Short lessons (15-20 min for young children)
- Nature study and outdoor time
- Narration (child retells what they learned)
- Habit training
- Wide and generous curriculum (no "core subjects" hierarchy)

### Section 2: Charlotte Mason and Open Education

**Key points:**
- CM is inherently "open" - it's about principles, not products
- The philosophy predates the curriculum industry that grew around it
- You can apply CM principles with any books, any content
- Mix and match: CM nature study + interest-led learning + structured math
- The "feast" concept - expose children to many things, see what they're drawn to

**Why CM fits OpenEd:**
- Philosophy over products
- Flexibility built in
- Works with library books, secular living books, nature in your backyard

### Section 3: Finding Charlotte Mason Through OpenEd

**What OpenEd can fund:**
- Living books (secular titles)
- Nature study supplies (field guides, nature journals, art supplies)
- Art and music appreciation resources
- Museum memberships
- Tutoring for subjects that need structure

**What OpenEd cannot fund:**
- Religious curricula (Simply Charlotte Mason, Ambleside Online, The Alveary are all Christian)

### Section 4: FAQ

- Do I need to buy a curriculum?
- How is CM different from unschooling?
- What ages does CM work for?

## Key Concept: Twaddle

Charlotte Mason coined the term "twaddle" for watered-down, dumbed-down content that talks down to children. Textbooks that oversimplify. Abridged versions that strip the life out of literature. Bright colors and cartoon characters substituting for substance.

The antidote is "living books" - real literature written by someone passionate about the subject, not a committee producing content for a grade level.

This concept is central to understanding why CM families care so much about book selection.

---

## Positioning: CM vs. Classical vs. Unschooling

**Charlotte Mason vs. Classical:**
- Both value great books and liberal arts
- Classical emphasizes the Trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric stages)
- CM rejects the "stages" idea - children are capable of sophisticated thinking at any age
- Classical can be drill-heavy (Latin conjugations, memorization)
- CM emphasizes short lessons, narration, nature over worksheets
- Classical often feels more rigorous/academic; CM feels more gentle/organic

**Charlotte Mason vs. Unschooling:**
- CM has more structure (scheduled lessons, required subjects)
- But CM shares belief that children learn through interest and experience
- CM's "wide and generous curriculum" = exposure to many things (like unschooling's "strewing")
- The "feast" concept overlaps with interest-led learning
- Many families blend both: CM structure for some subjects, child-led for others

## CM in Practice: Specific Examples

### Philosophy on Math and Nature
**Source:** Simply Charlotte Mason Podcast

"Anytime you're taking something that's chaotic and you're bringing order to it, that's math."

The podcast encourages parents to notice math in everyday moments - bird calls, photography rules, organizing shelves. "We don't have to consider ourselves mathematicians to be able to enjoy math."

Link: [The Beauty of Math](https://simplycharlottemason.com/blog/the-beauty-of-math/)

### Writing & Composition
**Source:** Brandy Vencel's Essay Writing Guide

Rooted in Mason's insight: "Composition should not be taught; it should be the outcome of spontaneous interest."

The plan blends daily narration, creative prompts, and real accountability to ensure essay writing does not feel like punishment.

### Art Appreciation / Picture Study
**Source:** Rebecca at A Humble Place - Albert Bierstadt Picture Study

Charlotte Mason understood that children learn art through direct observation. The method: show a child a great painting for 5-10 minutes, let them look, then ask what they notice. Builds "visual vocabulary."

Link: [Bierstadt Picture Study](https://ahumbleplace.com/product/bierstadt-picture-study/)

### Reading Aloud - The Cliffhanger Technique
**Source:** Simply Charlotte Mason - 10 Tips for Reading Aloud

"Stop at the good parts" rather than reading until a chapter ends. This builds anticipation, boosts creativity as imaginations run wild, and teaches patience.

Link: [10 Tips for Reading Aloud](https://simplycharlottemason.com/blog/10-tips-for-reading-aloud/)

### Affordable Homeschooling
**Source:** Simply Charlotte Mason - Charlotte Mason Without Breaking the Bank

Addresses the misconception that a rich education requires a large budget. Demonstrates how to provide a rich, literature-based education using library books, smart sharing, and free online resources rather than expensive curricula.

Link: [Charlotte Mason Without Breaking the Bank](https://simplycharlottemason.com/blog/charlotte-mason-without-breaking-the-bank/)

## OpenEd Community Voices

### Meg Thomas (coachmegthomas.com)
OpenEd mom, homeschool coach, mother of seven. Big following in the community.

**On the "Plutarch" Rule - Don't Force Classics:**
Meg attended a Charlotte Mason class where she was told, "Everybody needs to read Plutarch." She tried to implement this because she thought she "had to," but admitted: "I hated it. They [the kids] hated it. And like they don't even remember it... because I didn't really value those things."

**The Lesson:** When you identify what you value as a family, it helps you prioritize what truly matters over what is just "expected" by a specific educational philosophy.

**Heart, Habits, Home Framework:**
Meg outlines a three-part framework for success: Heart (connection), Habits, and Home (culture). Building strong habits allows the home to run smoothly, which makes the academics easier.

**Household Habits First:**
Before focusing heavily on curriculum, Meg taught her children habits to help the home run, such as doing laundry together as a "full family event." She notes: "I didn't realize that these skills I was teaching them was going to help me to make homeschool feasible long-term because I basically am working myself out of a job."

### Matt Bowman (Founder of OpenEd)
**The 20-20-20 Method:**
Matt suggests a simplified schedule: "20 minutes of focused math, 20 minutes of reading, [and] 20 minutes of writing," with one hour of free exploration or recess inserted between these blocks.

**Exhaust the Body First:**
Matt notes that you need an "exhausted body" (through play or recess) to have a mind open to learning math for 20 minutes. He advises against recreating the 40 or 50-minute bell cycle of traditional schools at home.

**Connection to CM:** Charlotte Mason's short lessons philosophy aligns perfectly with this approach.
