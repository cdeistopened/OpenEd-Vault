Classical Education for Elite Boys (1500s–1800s)

Classical education in early modern Europe and the United States was an educational paradigm centered on the languages, literature, and values of ancient Greece and Rome. It was predominantly reserved for elite boys and aimed to cultivate learned, virtuous gentlemen equipped for leadership in church, state, or intellectual life. Formal instruction typically began in childhood (often around age 7 in many regions ￼) and progressed through a rigorous curriculum of Latin and (later or more rarely) Greek, with liberal arts subjects built on this linguistic foundation. The Renaissance humanist movement had rekindled the ideal that by mastering classical languages and texts, students would absorb the civic virtues and wisdom of antiquity ￼. Across different countries – whether in Britain’s grammar schools, France’s collèges, Germany’s Gymnasien, or the American colonial Latin schools – classical education shared common features but also varied in structure, starting age, and pedagogical style. Below is a detailed overview by region, followed by a special focus on the Jesuit model and a comparative analysis of differences and goals.

British Education: Grammar Schools and Private Tutors

An illustration of a traditional English grammar school classroom (19th-century depiction, similar to earlier centuries). Such classrooms, often attached to parish or town institutions, changed little from Tudor times through the 18th century, emphasizing rote learning of Latin grammar and classical texts.

In England (and later Britain) from the 1500s through 1800s, elite boys typically received a blend of home-based tutoring and formal schooling in grammar schools. Formal education often began with a petty school or home tutor around age 5–7, where basics of reading (often using a hornbook) and religious catechism were learned ￼ ￼. By around age 7–9, boys who excelled could move on to a Latin grammar school, the core institution of classical education ￼. At grammar school, the curriculum revolved around Latin – mastering Latin grammar, vocabulary, and translation – with some schools introducing Greek at higher levels (though Greek was taught “much more rarely” in Elizabethan times) ￼. Classical literature (e.g. Virgil, Cicero, Homer) and the Bible (in Latin) were staple texts, occasionally supplemented by works of Renaissance humanists like Erasmus ￼. A few advanced grammar schools or private tutors even taught Hebrew (especially in Puritan contexts) as a tertiary classical language ￼.

Instructional methods in British grammar schools were typically rigorous and repetitive. Boys spent long hours in school – a schedule from 16th-century England shows classes beginning at 6 AM, breaking at midday, then continuing until 4 or 5 PM ￼. Much learning was by rote memorization of grammar rules and recitation of Latin paradigms. Translating Latin sententiae and classical passages back and forth between Latin and English was a daily exercise ￼. Competition was encouraged, often underpinned by fear of punishment: discipline was strict and corporal punishment (the birch cane) was a routine tool to enforce diligence ￼ ￼. Older boys might serve as ushers or assistant teachers to younger boys, reinforcing their own learning by teaching the basics to juniors ￼. Despite occasional progressive voices (Erasmus himself questioned dull drilling methods), the prevailing approach “taught the subject and not the child,” emphasizing visible results in language mastery over gentler developmental pedagogy ￼.

Social setting and duration: Grammar schools were often town-based and could enroll both local day students and boarders (boarders, called “tablers,” paid for meals and lodging) ￼. While open in theory to any who could pay (and some endowed “free schools” existed), in practice these schools served the gentry and professional classes; girls were generally excluded beyond basic literacy. School fees (perhaps a few pence per day, adding up to £20 a year) made grammar schooling a significant expense, so only well-off families sustained a boy’s education to the upper forms ￼. Students commonly left grammar school by age 14 or 15 once they had a solid grounding in Latin, though some stayed on until 17 or 18 to prepare for university entrance ￼. Outside the formal school system, wealthy families often hired private tutors to educate their sons at home. In fact, for the highest aristocracy (e.g. ducal or princely families), it was preferred that the heir be tutored privately rather than sent to any school where he might mix with lower-status boys ￼ ￼. A tutor’s curriculum for an aristocratic boy still centered on Latin and Greek, but often included modern languages (French was essential for diplomacy), history, geography, mathematics, fencing, music, and genteel accomplishments – all tailored to prepare the young noble for his future duties ￼. The philosophical goal was as much about character and leadership as about academics: an heir was to learn virtue, proper manners, and the skills to manage estates and political affairs ￼. As one account notes, the education of an heir focused on “duties and roles, virtues and morals, behavior and manners,” with scholastic studies (languages, rhetoric, etc.) seen as underpinning the ability to lead and converse effectively ￼.

By the 18th century, Britain’s elite educational pattern evolved into a combination of boarding schools (the famous “public schools” like Eton, Harrow, Winchester, etc.) and continued use of private tutors. The typical age of entry to boarding schools like Eton was around 13, though before the 1830s this was not standardized and many boys entered earlier or later ￼ ￼. (One source notes that in the Georgian era, enrollments at Eton ranged from as young as 7 or 8 up to mid-teens ￼.) These schools provided a classical curriculum comparable to grammar schools – intensive Latin and Greek study remained their “bread and butter” even into the 19th century, alongside some math, literature, and eventually science ￼. Discipline in 18th/19th-century English public schools was famously severe and student life spartan, aiming to build toughness and character. Indeed, by the 19th century, British classical education incorporated ideals of character-building (“muscular Christianity”), using sports and prefect systems to mold future imperial administrators, though those aspects were only beginning to take shape in the late 1700s. University was the capstone for many: Oxford and Cambridge (entirely classical in their entrance requirements and curricula) were the preserve of those aiming at the clergy or elite careers. Not all gentlemen pursued university, but those who did usually matriculated in their late teens (14–18 was common in the 16th–17th centuries ￼; by the  eighteenth century, entering at 16–18 was typical). At Cambridge or Oxford, students continued with the seven liberal arts (grammar, rhetoric, logic, geometry, arithmetic, music, astronomy) and advanced classical studies, alongside theology or law, in courses lasting 3–4 years for a bachelor’s degree ￼. The guiding humanist philosophy – at both school and university – was that classical learning instilled civic virtue and practical wisdom. Even as studies became more secular by Elizabethan times, the idea was that knowledge of Latin and Greek authors would help form wise, moral statesmen “armed with…civic values which would allow them to best serve their careers and the state” ￼.

In summary, British elite education from 1500s–1800s started with early literacy at home, proceeded through Latin grammar schooling or private tutoring in youth, and culminated for some in the residential public schools and ancient universities. Throughout, the content was steeped in the Classics and the aim was to produce well-rounded gentlemen – fluent in classical tongues, conversant with ancient literature and history, firm in character and piety, and prepared either to govern, to serve in clergy, or simply to take their place as educated members of the ruling class.

French Education: Collèges and Lycées for the Elite

In France during the 16th–18th centuries, classical education for boys was delivered mainly through institutions called collèges (not to be confused with modern “college,” these were roughly equivalent to secondary grammar schools). Many of these collèges were run by religious orders – notably the Jesuits (until their suppression in 1760s) and the Oratorians – or by Catholic dioceses and universities. Formal schooling often began a little later in France than in England; elementary instruction (in basic reading, writing, and catechism) might be obtained at a petite école or parish school in boyhood, but by age 10 or so talented boys of the elite would enter a collège for a multi-year program of Latin humanist education ￼. By 1710, France had approximately 360 such collèges, typically six- to eight-year programs that enrolled boys roughly from age 10 up to 17–20 ￼. These schools collectively educated on the order of 50,000 young men – a significant but still elite fraction of the youth – in the classical curriculum ￼.

Curriculum and methods: The French collège curriculum closely mirrored the pan-European humanist model. Latin was the core subject, taught through grammar drills, reading of classical authors, and composition in Latin. Students progressed through structured classes (often named sixième, cinquième, quatrième, etc. up to rhétorique and philosophie in the final years). Greek was taught in the upper forms of the best schools, and brilliant students would read Homer or Plato in the original. Classes in rhetoric (the art of persuasive speaking and writing) crowned the later years – reflecting the goal of training eloquent, learned young men. Additionally, the top class often called philosophie introduced basic scholastic philosophy, logic, and sometimes sciences. For example, an ordinance from Napoléon’s era (1808) fixed the lycée syllabus as including “ancient languages, history, rhetoric, logic, music, and the elements of mathematical and physical sciences” ￼. This shows that by the early 19th century, even as France reformed education, Latin and Greek remained central but were supplemented by math and science. Indeed, under Napoleon (First Empire), the French state re-established elite secondary schools called lycées (from 1802 onward) which were essentially revamped classical colleges. They taught French and classical languages plus modern subjects, and were usually boarding schools run with military-like discipline ￼. The Napoleonic lycée system aimed to produce a new generation of officials and officers, combining the traditional classical studies with Enlightenment-informed sciences – a slightly broader curriculum than the old monastic collèges.

During the 17th–18th centuries, teaching style in French classical schools was often shaped by the Catholic pedagogy. Jesuit and Oratorian teachers were highly educated clergy who used methods outlined in the Ratio Studiorum (for Jesuit colleges) – emphasizing structured progression, frequent examinations, theatrical exercises (student debates or Latin plays), and a balance of encouragement with discipline. Memorization and recitation were as important in France as elsewhere: boys would memorize Latin vocabularies, classical orations, and versify in Latin. One hallmark of the Jesuit collèges was the use of academic competitions and prizes to motivate students, in addition to corporal punishment when necessary. French schools generally maintained strict discipline (the piquet or confinement and caning were not unknown), but Jesuit schools, in particular, prided themselves on cultivating student loyalty and piety to minimize severe punishment. Most collèges were residential or had options for boarders, bringing together sons of nobles and bourgeois from a wide region. Notably, many French collegians received scholarships (bursaries) from the school’s endowments – by 18th century, most collèges had endowments from benefactors or the Crown that could fund poor but talented boys ￼. This indicates a commitment (at least in theory) to educating gens de talent regardless of wealth, although in practice the student body skewed toward the well-off and urbane.

Starting age and length: As mentioned, French boys typically entered a collège around age 10–12. The course length varied; some did a full eight-year course (roughly equivalent to modern secondary plus two years of advanced study), whereas others might do a shorter six-year version ￼. It was not unusual for a boy to finish the standard humanities curriculum by around age 17 or 18. Those aiming for higher studies (university faculties of law, medicine, or theology) would continue on to those at about age 18–20. However, the French universities of the Old Regime were often in disarray or had a narrow focus (many were primarily for professional degrees in law/medicine). Thus, the collège education was in many cases the pinnacle of a young elite’s schooling – a self-contained liberal arts training. After the Revolution, the disruption of church-run schools led to a temporary vacuum (many collèges closed or lost funding when revolutionary government seized church properties in the 1790s ￼). Napoleon’s lycée system in the early 1800s restored a centralized, state-controlled classical education, ensuring that the classical tradition persisted into the 19th century albeit under secular and standardized management ￼.

Philosophical goals: French classical education was intended to form both good Christians and able public men. In the Catholic monarchical context of the 16th–18th centuries, that meant fostering religious orthodoxy, eloquence, and knowledge of Latin culture to serve either in the Church or royal administration. An educated noble or bourgeois in France was expected to quote Latin authors, know ancient history, and uphold moral virtues extolled by classical and Christian teachings. After the Revolution, the rhetoric shifted – the state wanted education to instill patriotism and secular virtue, but it still leaned on classical models. For instance, revolutionary reformers like Condorcet planned universal schooling that included humanistic studies (though their plans were cut short) ￼. Napoleon explicitly geared lycées toward producing loyal, capable civil servants: the inclusion of modern sciences alongside Latin and Greek was meant to balance classical moral training with practical knowledge for bureaucracy or military. Nonetheless, the enduring ideal of the French lycée (which persisted through the 1800s) was a person steeped in belles-lettres – literature, ancient languages, philosophy – on the assumption that this formed the most rational, articulate, and morally grounded citizens ￼. In comparison to Britain, French schools were more tightly controlled by either Church or State, and schooling had a more official role in shaping ideology (e.g. during the Third Republic late 1800s, schools became a battleground between secular republican values and Catholic traditionalism ￼). But through the 1500s–1800s, despite political shifts, the content of elite education in France remained overwhelmingly classical and humanistic.

German Education: Latin Schools and the Gymnasium Tradition

In the German-speaking lands (Holy Roman Empire and succeeding states like Prussia) from the 16th to 19th centuries, elite boys likewise followed a classical curriculum, institutionalized in the form of Latin schools and later the modern Gymnasium. The Protestant Reformation gave a strong impulse to education: Reformers like Luther and Melanchthon advocated schooling so that all (boys) could read scripture, and this led many Protestant cities and principalities to found or expand Latin schools in the 1500s. The term Gymnasium in this context simply meant an advanced secondary school for classical studies – analogous to grammar schools or collèges elsewhere ￼ ￼. The Gymnasium model was famously shaped by Johann Sturm’s school in Strasbourg (1538), which became a prototype blending a full Latin curriculum with elements of higher learning ￼. Throughout the 17th–18th centuries, German Gymnasien (or Akademien in some cases) were centers of humanist learning for the urban elite and served as preparatory schools for university and the clergy.

Structure and starting age: A German boy destined for higher learning typically attended a local Grundschule or parish school in early childhood to learn reading, writing, and catechism (often from age 6 to 9). At around age 9 or 10, he would enter the Gymnasium if he showed academic promise ￼. The Gymnasium course was long – often around 8 or 9 years, taking the student through adolescence. By the 19th century in Prussia, the Gymnasium was formally a nine-year course for ages ~10–18, culminating in the Abitur examination (or Maturität), which was required for university admission ￼. (An official 1812 Prussian regulation declared that only schools titled “Gymnasium” could send students on to university, cementing the Gymnasium as the college-prep track ￼.) Early on, some Latin schools were shorter or lacked upper grades, but the trend over these centuries was toward a comprehensive curriculum under one roof – by the late 18th century a Gymnasium would typically have lower grades focused on grammar and upper grades on humanities and philosophy.

Curriculum: Latin was the undisputed foundation. In the 1500s–1600s, students in German Latin schools would spend years drilling Latin grammar (often using the textbooks by Donatus or later Manuel Alvares, a standard Jesuit Latin grammar), reading classical authors, and writing compositions. Instruction was conducted largely in Latin – Latin was the language of academia, law, and government paperwork in many German states well into the 18th century, so fluency was essential ￼ ￼. Greek was also taught, especially in Protestant schools where reading the New Testament in Greek was valued. However, emphasis on Greek became even stronger in the late 18th-century “Neohumanist” movement. German educators around 1770–1810 (figures like Wilhelm von Humboldt) championed Greek language and literature as the purest source of humanistic Bildung ￼ ￼. Thus, while 17th-century curricula might have treated Greek as optional or second-tier, by the 19th century the Humanistisches Gymnasium required extensive study of both Latin and Ancient Greek (along with some Hebrew if preparing for theology). In addition to languages, the German gymnasium curriculum covered the broad liberal arts. Students studied rhetoric, poetics, history, geography, math, and philosophy in the upper forms. A typical 18th-century syllabus in a Gymnasium included Aristotelian logic and ethics, some geometry and astronomy (remnants of the medieval quadrivium), and perhaps elements of natural science, though classical philology dominated ￼. By the early 19th century, Prussian reforms explicitly added modern subjects: modern foreign languages (French, and later English), more mathematics, and natural sciences were introduced alongside the classical languages ￼. This broadened curriculum reflected an effort to create “harmonious cultivation of the mind” as Humboldt put it – combining ancient and modern knowledge to produce well-rounded graduates ￼.

Teaching style and environment: German gymnasia, whether Catholic or Protestant, tended to be academically rigorous and structured by class level. Teachers (often Lutheran ministers or Jesuit priests, depending on region/confession) were well-educated and the prestige of teaching was comparatively high. Class sizes could be large – one report from the late 19th century notes classes of 30–50 were common and sometimes up to 100 in lecture portions ￼ ￼, though that source refers to Jesuit practice elsewhere, German schools too could be crowded. Methods included a mix of memorization (e.g. Latin grammar rules), prelection (the teacher would read and explicate a Latin text, while students listened and took notes ￼), and regular oral and written examinations. Corporal punishment was used (the cane or rod was a universal tool in early modern schooling), but 18th-century Enlightenment influence and pietist religious influence (e.g. schools like August Hermann Francke’s in Prussia) encouraged more gentle moral discipline in some places. By the 19th century, Prussian schools were supervised by state authorities who issued detailed regulations on curricula and even pedagogy – the Lehrplan (curriculum plan) was considered “a sacred thing” and remarkably uniform across schools ￼ ￼. This centralization meant that in any Prussian Gymnasium, a given grade would be studying the same Latin author or math topic as another Gymnasium in a different city. Consistent with German bureaucratic organization, testing was formalized – the Abitur exam at the end tested Latin, Greek, math, etc., and only those who passed received the certificate allowing university entrance. The selectivity of the Gymnasien was notable: even by the late 1800s, only about 3% of German youth attended a Gymnasium (and under 1% went on to university) ￼, meaning it was an elite route primarily for sons of the educated middle class (Bildungsbürgertum) and nobility.

Goals and ideals: The purpose of classical education in Germany evolved over the period but consistently intertwined scholarly erudition with moral formation. In the 16th–17th centuries, the Latin school/Gymnasium was intended to train future clergymen, jurists, and officials – essentially to perpetuate a “learned estate” of Latin-educated leaders ￼. Students were often the sons of city councillors, professors, pastors, or prosperous merchants who needed Latin for their trade. As the Enlightenment and Neohumanismus took hold, German educators reframed the goal as Bildung – the cultivation of the individual’s mind and character through contact with the great works of literature and thought ￼. The ancient classics, especially Greek poetry and philosophy, were seen as vehicles to develop aesthetic appreciation, critical reason, and a strong ethical compass. After the French Revolution, there was also a patriotic element: a classically-educated bourgeoisie was to become the backbone of a reformed, robust nation (this was especially true in Prussia after 1806, where educational reform was part of recovering from military defeat). Humboldt’s 1810s reforms explicitly made the Gymnasium a tool for creating enlightened citizens and able civil servants, not just clergymen. Thus by the 19th century, the Gymnasium ideal was that a young man spends nearly a decade in deep study of Latin, Greek, mathematics, history, etc., emerging at 18 with a “certificate of maturity” (Abitur literally signifies maturity) indicating both intellectual and personal maturity ￼. With this formation, he could enter university to specialize or go directly into public service. To summarize, German classical education aimed to transmit the intellectual heritage of antiquity and to instill disciplined thinking and virtue – “the new ideals of humanity and aesthetic purity” were believed to be found in the Classics, and mastering them was the rite of passage into the educated class ￼.

It’s worth noting that by the late 1800s, Germany (and neighboring countries influenced by its model) also developed alternative schools – e.g. Realgymnasium (teaching Latin and modern languages but no Greek) and Oberrealschule (no Latin/Greek, all modern subjects) – reflecting debates over the practicality of classical studies ￼. However, through most of our period (1500s–1800s) the traditional classical Gymnasium held primacy, and knowledge of Latin (and often Greek) remained a hallmark of the Bildungselite. A student who completed this education had not only linguistic skills but also a shared cultural canon – from Caesar and Cicero to Homer and Sophocles – which was supposed to guide his ethics and civic life. In fact, well into the 19th century many German universities required proof of Latin and Greek (Latinum and Graecum certificates) for entry into fields like law, medicine, or theology ￼, underscoring how classical education was intertwined with all higher learning.

Education in Colonial and Early America: The New World Adaptation

Classical education crossed the Atlantic with the settlers and remained the standard for educating elite boys in the American colonies and early United States (17th–early 19th century). The New England Puritans, in particular, placed strong emphasis on literacy and scriptural study, which dovetailed with classical training. The very first secondary school in the English colonies, Boston Latin School (founded 1635), was a direct transplant of the English grammar school model and focused on Latin and Greek from its inception. Generally, in 17th–18th century America, formal schooling began early – basic reading was taught at home or in small local “dame schools” from about age 5 onward ￼. By ages 7–8, boys who were to receive advanced education might attend a grammar school (often called a Latin school). These colonial grammar schools were essentially identical in curriculum to their English counterparts: “The curriculum was heavily weighted to Latin and Greek, plus some mathematics”, as one summary puts it ￼. Students drilled Latin grammar (using texts like Lily’s Grammar or later American reprints), read classical authors, and learned some Greek to handle the New Testament and classical Greek works. One hallmark of Puritan education was the inclusion of Hebrew study as well – since ministers needed to read the Old Testament in the original tongue, some grammar schools taught basic Hebrew alongside Latin and Greek ￼. Overall, colonial elite education was explicitly designed to prepare boys for college (university) and largely for the ministry. Harvard College (founded 1636) set the template: its entrance requirements in the 17th century demanded proficiency in Latin and knowledge of Greek. This standard persisted; for example, in the 18th century Harvard and other colonial colleges like Yale required applicants to be able to read classical texts in the original languages. John Adams and John Hancock, for instance, attended Harvard where “admissions required fluent reading of Cicero and ease in Latin and Greek” ￼. In practice, this meant a boy headed for Harvard would, by his mid-teens, be reading authors like Vergil, Cicero, and the Greek gospels in school (or with a tutor). Indeed, many Founding Fathers had intense classical training: Alexander Hamilton, entering King’s College (Columbia) in 1773, could already read several of Cicero’s orations and Virgil’s Aeneid in Latin and translate the Gospel of John from Greek ￼; James Madison had read deeply in Latin literature (Virgil, Horace, Tacitus, etc.) before attending Princeton ￼.

Educational institutions: In New England, towns were mandated (by laws such as Massachusetts’ 1647 “Old Deluder Satan Act”) to establish a grammar school once they reached a certain size ￼. These were usually all-male schools aimed at preparing local boys for Harvard or for leadership. Such grammar schools were typically small (perhaps one master and a handful of pupils) and funding was a mix of town funds and tuition. The History.com article notes that in colonial Massachusetts, a town grammar school charged tuition, but sometimes accepted payment in kind (goods) and often relied on local taxes or charity for support ￼ ￼. In the Middle Colonies (New York, Pennsylvania, etc.), education was more often provided by church-run or private academies, and had a bit more diversity (including some practical subjects) due to influences of Quakers and others, but classical learning was still a marker of the elite. In the South, formal schools were fewer; planter families often hired private tutors for their sons or sent them to England for schooling. For instance, George Washington did not attend a grammar school long (his formal education was limited), but wealthy Virginians like Thomas Jefferson studied under tutors (Jefferson learned Latin, Greek, and French from clergyman tutors in his youth ￼ ￼). Several Southern colonies eventually founded colleges (e.g. the College of William & Mary in 1693, King’s College in New York in 1754, etc.), and to feed these, private academies or “colleges” (meaning secondary schools) were established in the 18th century. By the early U.S. (post-1776) period, there was a proliferation of academy-style schools (often called “Latin academies” or just academies) which combined classical curricula with some newer subjects. For example, Benjamin Franklin in mid-18th century advocated for an Academy in Philadelphia that would teach English, modern subjects, and classical languages – reflecting some push to broaden education. But the dominant expectation remained that any boy aspiring to the higher professions must study Latin and Greek in his youth.

Curriculum and methods in colonial schools: A contemporary description of a colonial Latin school would closely resemble that of an English grammar school. The New England Primer and Bible were used in early reading education for basic literacy ￼. Once in grammar school, the boy plunged into Latin. Typical routine: memorizing Latin grammar rules (perhaps from Cheever’s Latin Accidence), reciting conjugations and declensions, translating Latin sentences to English and back. Canings for mistakes were common – Puritan schoolmasters were as strict as their Old World counterparts, if not more so. An observer noted that in these schools, “students would mostly memorize and recite passages” – rote learning was the order of the day ￼. School days were long when farm work didn’t intervene (though outside of towns, many pupils attended only part-time, especially in planting or harvest seasons) ￼. By the late teens, a diligent student would have read portions of classical histories, poetry, and orations. For those headed to college, there was an expectation to also grasp some basic arithmetic and geometry, though many college entrants in the 1700s had only rudimentary math compared to their fluency in Latin. Notably, the philosophical and civic goals of American classical education mirrored the European: New England Puritans saw it as essential for raising knowledgeable clergymen and magistrates who could govern morally. Literacy was cast as a religious duty (“for Puritans, reading was a religious duty” because it enabled Bible study ￼). This religious imperative fueled the establishment of schools at a higher rate in New England than elsewhere. By the Revolutionary period, classical schooling also took on a patriotic dimension. The American Founders believed that understanding Greek and Roman history and republican ideals was vital to building the new nation. They consciously drew lessons from antiquity: for instance, the Federalist Papers assumed readers knew references to ancient republics, and the likes of Madison and Hamilton invoked Greek and Roman examples in political debate ￼. Thus, a classical education was seen as cultivating civic virtue – the ideal of the citizen who, like a Roman senator or Athenian citizen, could put the common good first. Thomas Jefferson argued for broad liberal education to nurture an informed citizenry, saying that an educated and virtuous populace was the only safe guardian of the American republic ￼. In practice, of course, only the male gentry received full classical schooling; women and lower-class boys had very limited access to advanced education in this era.

By the early 19th century, the United States slowly began to diversify curricula (the rise of public schools and West Point’s focus on engineering, for example, introduced more math/science). But well into the 1800s, the old classical model dominated preparatory education. The Ivy League colleges required Latin and Greek for admission until late in the 19th century. An anecdote: as late as 1828, Yale issued a famous report defending the classical curriculum as the best way to discipline the mind, highlighting the staying power of Latin and Greek in American elite education. In sum, colonial and early U.S. education for elite boys was a transplant of European classical schooling with a Puritan and later republican flavor – it aimed to produce learned ministers, lawyers, and statesmen steeped in the Classics and capable of both moral leadership and intellectual discourse.

The Jesuit Educational Model

A depiction of an elite tutoring session: Francis Ayscough (right) tutoring the young Prince of Wales (future King George III) and his brother, 1740s. Jesuit colleges similarly often provided personalized instruction to noble youth, aiming to form both their intellect and character (Richard Wilson, public domain).

No discussion of classical education in 1500s–1800s is complete without examining the Jesuits, whose teaching institutions set the gold standard (and sometimes the lightning rod for controversy) in early modern education. The Society of Jesus (Jesuits), a Catholic order founded in 1540, rapidly became renowned for its network of colleges across Catholic Europe (and beyond). By leveraging a highly organized pedagogy – codified in the Ratio atque Institutio Studiorum Societatis Iesu of 1599 (known as the Ratio Studiorum) – the Jesuits established hundreds of schools that educated the sons of the Catholic elites (and often talented commoners) in a rigorous classical curriculum ￼ ￼. One hundred years after their founding, the Jesuits were running over 400 schools; by 1739, some 669 Jesuit institutions were in operation worldwide ￼, a testament to their pervasive role in education.

Curriculum and structure: Jesuit colleges were typically secondary schools (sometimes with additional higher-level classes) that accepted boys who had already learned basic reading and catechism at home or elementary school – the Ratio Studiorum “did not contain provisions for elementary education,” focusing on secondary and higher studies ￼. The Jesuit program was divided generally into three main stages: grammar, humanities, and rhetoric. According to one summary, “the Jesuits began educating students (mostly of middle-school age) with 3 years of grammar, transition to 1 year of humanities, and then end with rhetoric” ￼. In practice, this corresponded to roughly five grades of the lower school (often called Grammar grades: lowest was sometimes “rudiments” followed by grammar levels often named infima, grammatical, syntaxis, and humanitas) and two higher-level years (usually rhetoric and philosophy). The grammar stage focused on Latin fundamentals: grammar drills, Latin composition, and basic readings (e.g. fables, Erasmus’ dialogues). Greek was introduced usually after a couple of years of Latin; by the humanities year, students read Greek and Latin authors more extensively. The rhetoric class (approximately for ages 15–16) was the capstone where students read the great orators (Cicero, Demosthenes), poets (Virgil, Homer), and practiced eloquence through orations, debates, and drama ￼ ￼. Some Jesuit colleges also had a philosophy course (covering logic, natural philosophy, metaphysics) for the oldest students, which made their schools resemble a combined high school & junior college. The Ratio Studiorum prescribed in detail which authors to cover and how: for instance, Cicero and Aristotle in rhetoric/philosophy, and in lower levels smaller classical works and humanist texts. It “relied on the classical subjects – theology, philosophy, Latin and Greek” as its core, and even when revised in 1832, it still built on those while adding a bit more native language, history, geography, math, and science in response to modern needs ￼.

Teaching methods: Jesuit pedagogy was famously systematic and student-engaging. Classes usually began with a prelection: the teacher would read a short passage from a Latin (or Greek) text slowly, explain its content, vocabulary, and grammar points (students did not take notes during the prelection, they were to listen attentively) ￼. After this, students might be called on to recite memorized grammar rules or to translate bits of the passage. The Ratio encouraged creating academic rivalry in a friendly way – students were often divided into two teams (Romans and Carthaginians, for example) and earned points for their team by answering questions well. Regular examinations and promotions were held; the best students could be promoted to a higher class mid-year if merited. Another distinctive feature was the emphasis on drama and oratory: Jesuit schools had students put on Latin plays (often on classical or biblical themes) and practice declamations. The Ratio mentions that parts of the curriculum included drama (comedy and tragedy) in Latin, since if students could act and speak powerfully in Latin, it proved they had truly mastered the language ￼ ￼. The goal was to build confidence, memory, and a “presenter’s persona” before an audience ￼. Similarly, disputation was used in upper classes – students might have to argue opposing sides of a thesis in Latin, sharpening their reasoning.

Discipline in Jesuit schools was generally strict but tempered with moral and religious guidance. The Jesuits believed in forming character and piety alongside intellect. The Ratio Studiorum explicitly states the aim of education is to lead students to the knowledge and love of God ￼. A famous Jesuit maxim: “In order to form a Christian, they must first form a man”, emphasizing virtue development ￼. Teachers were exhorted to be caring and act in loco parentis – to love the students but also correct them firmly. The use of corporal punishment was allowed but with moderation and only by certain officials. The Jesuits also pioneered what we might call guidance counseling: the Prefect of Studies and Prefect of Discipline in each college monitored student progress and conduct, providing support or admonition as needed.

Another Jesuit hallmark was free education. Many Jesuit colleges charged no tuition, being funded by endowments or state subventions. This opened their doors to talented non-aristocrats (though nobles and wealthy bourgeois attended in large numbers too). For example, in late 16th century, “Jesuits founded their own schools and offered free training in Latin grammar, Philosophy, Theology, Geography, etc., for boys” as part of the Counter-Reformation effort to educate both laymen and future priests ￼. This was a novel idea at the time – providing high-quality education at no direct cost – and it made Jesuit schools extremely popular and competitive.

Geographical scope: The Jesuit model was implemented not only in Catholic strongholds like France, Spain, Italy, and the Catholic German states, but also in places like Poland, Bohemia, and missions as far as the New World and Asia. Wherever they went, Jesuit educators took the Ratio Studiorum as a blueprint, yielding a remarkably consistent curriculum across continents. This consistency helped make “Jesuit education” a brand associated with academic excellence and rigorous classical training. Even Protestants acknowledged (sometimes grudgingly) the effectiveness of Jesuit pedagogy; many non-Catholic schools in the 17th century started imitating certain Jesuit methods (like periodic class rankings and prize ceremonies).

Goals: The philosophical and moral goals of Jesuit education were twofold: to produce devout, learned young men who would be loyal to the Catholic Church and to equip them for leadership roles in society (as clergymen or lay leaders). A Jesuit school graduate ideally embodied the Jesuit motto “Ad maiorem Dei gloriam” (For the greater glory of God) in his virtuous life, and also the humanist ideal of the eloquent, rational gentleman. The Jesuit curriculum stressed developing both virtue and intellect. The Ratio stated students should “improve in character, literary skills and learning in general” ￼. The Jesuits believed that a well-ordered mind, trained by the Classics, would also be a moral mind. They also placed importance on service: many Jesuit-taught alumni went on to serve in courts, governments, and armies, and the Jesuits took pride in influencing these men to act for the common good of both Church and state. In fact, Jesuit schools often educated the sons of the Catholic nobility (e.g. in France or Austria), effectively shaping the next generation of rulers. This raised suspicions among some secular authorities who feared Jesuit influence – but it attests to how deeply the Jesuit educational model penetrated the fabric of elite formation in the early modern period.

The Jesuit model faced a setback when the order was suppressed in 1773 (temporarily, until restoration in 1814) – during that hiatus, other orders or state officials tried to fill the gap using modified Jesuit curricula. The enduring legacy of the Jesuits in education is evident: even after 1773, the standardized classical curriculum they had championed remained the template in many places. In summary, the Jesuits exemplified the height of classical education – highly structured, thorough in classical languages, pedagogically innovative, and aimed at both intellectual and spiritual excellence. Their schools produced many of the leading scholars, churchmen, and statesmen of the 17th–18th centuries, thus deeply influencing the course of European education.

Comparative Differences and Philosophical Goals Across Regions

Classical education, while sharing a core devotion to Latin, Greek, and the liberal arts, was not monolithic. Different countries and periods introduced variations in starting age, curriculum content, teaching style, and underlying purpose. Below are some key differences and commonalities:
	•	Age of Starting and Duration: In most regions, formal classical schooling began around age 7–10, after basic literacy was acquired. English boys often started grammar school by 7–8 ￼, whereas French boys tended to enter collèges a bit later (around 10) ￼, and German boys entered Gymnasium about 9–10 ￼. Elite British public schools by the 18th century commonly admitted boys at 12–14 (though younger admissions occurred) ￼ ￼. Across the board, a full classical course lasted into the mid or late teens – e.g. finishing at 14–15 for many English grammar school boys ￼, or around 18 in a German Gymnasium or Napoleonic lycée ￼. Thus, while the starting point could differ slightly, the endpoint of producing a young man ready for university or society by about age 16–18 was common. Notably, university entry ages skewed younger in the 16th–17th centuries (14–16 was not unusual at Oxford/Cambridge ￼), but by the 19th century it normalized closer to 18–20 in many places.
	•	Curriculum Emphasis: All classical schools taught Latin grammar and literature as the central pillar. The breadth of curriculum varied:
	•	In England and colonial America, there was relatively little mathematics or science in the classical schools (these were considered separate or lower-priority; a grammar school might teach only modest arithmetic). The focus was squarely on language and moral literature.
	•	Germany, especially by the 19th century, integrated more math and science into the Gymnasium (Humboldt’s reforms added modern subjects to the previously classical-only syllabus ￼).
	•	France’s lycée from 1800s similarly included “elements of mathematical and physical sciences” alongside Latin/Greek ￼, whereas the old collège of the 1700s was almost purely classical.
	•	The presence of Greek was another differentiator: in 16th–17th century, not all schools taught Greek thoroughly (e.g. smaller English grammar schools “much more rarely” taught Greek ￼, and some German Lateinschulen omitted Greek). But by the late 18th century, Greek had a resurgence in curricula (thanks to Neohumanism and general Enlightenment interest in Greek democracy and art). German Gymnasien after 1780 and British public schools in the 19th century made Greek a staple subject alongside Latin. Puritan New England also gave attention to Greek (for scripture) and even Hebrew, which was unusual elsewhere ￼.
	•	Modern languages were largely absent from curricula until the late 18th–19th centuries. French nobility might learn Italian or Spanish from tutors for polite accomplishment, and British aristocrats learned French as the language of diplomacy, but these were often extracurricular. The formal school curriculum did not include modern vernacular literature study until the 1800s (for instance, Prussian reforms introduced German literature as a subject only after the Napoleonic period).
	•	Instructional Style and Discipline: Memorization, recitation, and corporal punishment were common to all regions in the 1500s–1700s. However, there were some nuanced differences:
	•	Jesuit schools employed more active learning strategies (drama, debates, team competitions) than some of their secular or Protestant counterparts, which leaned heavily on rote learning ￼. This made Jesuit classes somewhat more dynamic.
	•	English public schools by the 18th–19th century developed a prefect system and student “fagging” that made older boys partially enforce discipline – a unique cultural element of British boarding schools meant to instill leadership (albeit with much bullying). Continental schools did not have this self-governance system; discipline remained teacher-centered.
	•	The severity of discipline could also vary by cultural context: e.g., Puritan New England masters were famously strict (education was a grave moral duty), while some 18th-century Enlightenment-influenced teachers in Europe began to question excessive flogging. By early 19th century, one sees more emphasis in some German and French pedagogical writings on understanding student psychology and not just beating lessons into them – yet old habits died hard, and the rod remained a symbol of schooling almost everywhere until well into the 19th century ￼ ￼.
	•	Another difference was class size and individual attention: aristocratic children with private tutors (in England, France, etc.) obviously had personalized instruction – their education might include more field trips (Grand Tours, etc.) and practical learning (dancing, riding, conversation) than a large classroom could offer. On the other hand, a day boy in a Latin school might be one of 30 pupils of a single master. German states by 1800 had larger, more bureaucratic classes, whereas a small New England village Latin school might only have a handful of students spanning different ages in one room.
	•	Religious vs Secular orientation: All classical education before the late 18th century had a strong religious underpinning – the Bible (in Latin or Greek) was often a text in class, and moral instruction was intertwined. But differences existed:
	•	Protestant regions (England, northern Germany, New England) tended to use classical education to bolster Biblical literacy and Protestant doctrine (e.g., grammar schools in Elizabethan England still taught prayers and catechism alongside Latin ￼).
	•	Catholic education (France, Habsburg lands) under Jesuits aimed to reinforce Catholic orthodoxy and produce loyal churchmen.
	•	By the late 1700s, with the Enlightenment, secular or civic rationales for classical education grew. For example, the French Revolutionary government briefly de-emphasized religious content and tried to make education about producing enlightened citizens (Condorcet’s plan) ￼. Similarly, late 18th-century Prussian education incorporated Enlightenment values, although religion remained, especially in predominantly Protestant Prussia, a key subject (often one class period of Religion was mandatory even in 19th-century Gymnasium). The early U.S. was somewhat unique in tying classical learning to republican self-government, as described above, but that was really an extension of secular civic humanism which had existed in European thought for a while.
	•	Philosophical Goals: In essence, classical education was viewed as education for leadership. The nuances of that leadership ideal varied:
	•	Civic Virtue and Republicanism: Drawing from Renaissance humanism, educators everywhere believed classical history and literature taught moral examples. This was explicit in the training of statesmen. For example, the Founding Fathers’ classical education fueled their political philosophy about liberty and mixed government ￼ ￼. In Europe, even absolute monarchies wanted officials who admired Roman virtus and Greek wisdom in order to be effective servants of the state. The idea of civic virtue – that education should inculcate duty to the community – was a through-line from Italian city-states in 1500s (with their civic humanist schools) to American revolutionaries in 1770s.
	•	Preparation for University and Professions: A very practical goal was always present: these schools prepared boys for the next stage, whether that was university study of theology/law/medicine or direct entry into roles like court officer or military academy. Hence the stress on Latin (the language of law, diplomacy, and academia). In Germany, the Abitur guaranteed access to university and higher civil service, making the Gymnasium explicitly a pipeline to the professions ￼. In England, grammar schools were said to prepare students for college and ultimately for the clergy or learned professions ￼.
	•	Moral and Religious Formation: All systems saw moral upbringing as central. A Jesuit schoolmaster aimed to produce a devout Christian who was also intellectually adept ￼; a Puritan teacher aimed to form a godly, disciplined man who could resist Satan’s wiles (hence the name of the 1647 Act requiring schooling in Massachusetts ￼); a Church of England grammar school reinforced Anglican piety in the classics, etc. The balance of moral vs intellectual goals sometimes tipped one way or another – e.g. Pietist-influenced schools (like Francke’s in Halle, early 1700s) put special emphasis on personal piety and charitable outlook, whereas some Enlightenment-era teachers focused more on critical thinking skills and less on dogma. But generally, the ideal outcome was a well-rounded gentleman: virtuous, knowledgeable, articulate, and capable of reasoned judgment.
	•	National and Cultural Identity: Over time, classical education also became a marker of cultural identity. By the 19th century, to be gebildet (cultured) in Germany or cultivé in France or a member of the English gentlemanly class meant one had read one’s Virgil and Homer. This formed a kind of social glue among elites across Europe and America – a shared canon. Yet, each nation also bent the classical ideal to bolster its own identity: the French saw themselves as inheritors of Roman imperial glory and Greek taste; the British compared their empire with Rome’s; Germans in the 19th century idolized Greek philosophy and art as models for their own intellectual flowering. Thus, classical education was used to draw parallels between ancient virtues and contemporary aspirations. For instance, James Madison read about Athenian and Roman republics to help craft the American Constitution ￼, while a French Lycée student under Napoleon might read Caesar’s Gallic Wars and see Napoleon as a modern Caesar.

In conclusion, despite regional differences, classical education from the 1500s through 1800s maintained a remarkable continuity. Latin and Greek were the twin axes around which the curriculum revolved; grammar, rhetoric, and logic were the key skills imparted; and forming virtuous, learned leaders was the universal objective. Whether in a Jesuit college in Paris, a Lutheran Gymnasium in Prussia, or a small academy in Massachusetts, an elite boy’s schooling would be steeped in ancient languages and literature. This classical training was believed to sharpen the mind, improve moral reasoning, and connect the student to timeless knowledge – a preparation for whatever high calling (university scholar, clergyman, statesman, or enlightened landowner) his station in life entailed. The legacy of this era of education is profound: it produced generations of thinkers and leaders who, for better or worse, guided their societies with an ethos drawn from Cicero, Plato, and Scripture, all learned on the hard benches of the Latin school.

Today, Mortimer Adler’s impact persists not only through the ongoing work of the **Great Books Foundation** (which he co-founded to promote book discussion groups), but also in many classical and liberal arts programs. Numerous classical Christian schools and Great Books-based colleges _“across the globe bear Adler’s mark,”_ continuing his legacy of humane learning for students of all ages . In essence, Adler injected into the classical education revival a conviction that _what_ we read (and discuss) matters profoundly – that a true education must grapple with history’s best and most profound books and ideas, not just contemporary textbooks.

  

## **The Great Books Program in Classical Education**

  

In classical curricula, **Great Books** are more than just a reading list – they form the backbone of studying history, literature, philosophy, and even science through original works. The idea of a “Great Books program” typically means structuring courses around the careful reading and discussion of foundational classics of Western civilization. These are the works that have “stood the test of time” for their **beauty**, **eloquence**, historical impact, and profundity in addressing essential human questions . As one classical school founder explained, _“The Great Books are works that have stood the test of time … in addressing the essential questions of what does it mean to be a human being. What is justice? What is knowledge? What is my duty to myself, my family, my friends?”_ . By engaging students with authors like Plato, Aristotle, Augustine, Shakespeare, Jane Austen, and Frederick Douglass, classical programs aim to have students join what Hutchins and Adler termed the “Great Conversation” – the ongoing dialogue of great minds grappling with truth, ethics, and the human condition across the ages .

  

In practice, Great Books education can take various forms. At the collegiate level, a few institutions (such as **St. John’s College** in Annapolis/Santa Fe or Thomas Aquinas College in California) have famously adopted an all-Great-Books curriculum, where students read original texts exclusively and learn through Socratic seminars. In high school programs, “Great Books” often appears as a humanities sequence (sometimes called **Western Civilization** or **Literature of the Western World**) where students might read, say, _The Iliad_ and _Plato’s dialogues_ in 9th grade, _Dante_ and _Shakespeare_ in 10th, _Tolstoy_ and _Frederick Douglass_ in later years, and so on. Even younger students get a taste: a classical fifth-grader might read stories from _The Odyssey_ or simplified versions of _Gilgamesh_, while by ninth grade they tackle Homer’s _Iliad_ in the original English translation . The emphasis is on having students **struggle with the original sources** and ideas directly, rather than just using textbooks to summarize them. In many classical classrooms (and online discussion groups), you’ll find students actively debating the ideas in the _Great Books_, learning to support their views with evidence and to appreciate the depth of the authors’ insights. This content-rich approach contrasts with much of modern progressive education by unapologetically affirming that there _is_ a core body of knowledge and literature worth knowing . Proponents argue that reading the Great Books builds cultural literacy, cultivates moral and intellectual virtue, and trains the mind to analyze and think critically about perennial issues. As one commentator summarized, a Great Books program strives to _“give students the crucial knowledge and thinking skills they need for adult life — with an emphasis on exposure to, and grappling with, the great works of Western civilization.”_ In sum, the Great Books component of classical education is one of its more advanced and ambitious aspects – suited not only to child-friendly storytelling in the early years, but increasingly to rigorous, college-level discussion as students mature.

  

## **Classical Charter Schools and Virtual Academies**

  

One striking development in recent years is the rise of **classical** models in non-religious and public education settings. Notably, a growing number of public charter schools have adopted the classical curriculum, bringing Latin, logic, and great books to tuition-free public school students. For example, **Great Hearts Academies**—founded in Arizona and since expanded to Texas and other states—has become one of the largest networks of classical schools. Great Hearts now operates over 30 classical charter campuses and even a private classical academy; it describes itself as _“the leading provider of classical education in the country, with in-person public charter, online charter, and private academy offerings across multiple states.”_ These schools follow a liberal arts curriculum (often including daily literature from classic novels, philosophy in high school, instruction in Latin or Greek, and an emphasis on virtue education). Another initiative, Hillsdale College’s **Barney Charter School Initiative**, has helped launch dozens of classical charter schools in partnership with local communities, guided by Hillsdale’s curriculum framework (which famously emphasizes American founding documents, classical literature, and civics). This expansion has caught the attention of policymakers as well – for instance, in 2023 Florida’s governor touted classical charter schools (using Hillsdale’s model) as exemplars of a rich, content-based education in contrast to narrowly test-driven models .

  

Classical education has also entered the digital realm. **Great Hearts Online** is a fully online K–12 program offering the same classical liberal arts curriculum of Great Hearts’ brick-and-mortar schools, but to students learning from home. Started during the COVID-19 pandemic, Great Hearts Online provides live teacher-led classes and even asynchronous options, allowing families in certain states (Arizona, Texas, and expanding others) to access classical schooling tuition-free via technology . Similarly, the _Well-Trained Mind Academy_ (founded by Susan Wise Bauer) offers live online courses in all subjects for middle and high schoolers worldwide, functioning as a **virtual classical school** that can be used à la carte or as a full course load . This gives homeschool or remote students access to expert teachers in subjects like Latin, logic, ancient history, and literature that parents might not feel comfortable teaching on their own.

  

Internationally, the classical education revival is beginning to spread as well. In the UK, for example, 2013 saw the founding of **The King Alfred School** in Dudley, which is billed as “the flagship school for classical education in the UK” and the model for a new Classical British Education network . This school, like many of its American counterparts, combines a classical liberal arts curriculum with Christian values, and it has rapidly grown (planning to extend up to GCSE levels). Other countries have smaller but growing movements of parents and educators interested in reviving Latin, classical literature, and the trivium approach in their own schools or homeschools. While the movement remains most robust in the United States, such examples show a renewed global interest in educational methods that were common centuries ago but had largely fallen out of favor. Whether through local charter schools, private academies, or online platforms, classical education is finding new life and reaching a broader audience than ever before.

  

## **Modern Classical Education Resources and Tools**

  

For families and educators curious about classical education today, there is a wealth of resources available – from curriculum guides and books to interactive apps – that make this approach more accessible (and adaptable) than in the past. Below is an overview of notable resources, organized by type:

- **Guides and Core Books:** _The Well-Trained Mind_ by Susan Wise Bauer (5th ed., 2024) remains a cornerstone guide, offering a detailed, step-by-step **curriculum outline** from K–12 along with book lists and study tips . Another influential book is _The Liberal Arts Tradition_ (Kevin Clark & Ravi Jain, rev. 2019), which provides a philosophical framework for classical Christian education and is widely endorsed by leaders in the movement . For a secular perspective, E.D. Hirsch’s _Core Knowledge_ series (e.g., _What Your First Grader Needs to Know_) gives grade-by-grade content guidelines that complement classical content knowledge  . Parents and teachers often also read _How to Read a Book_ by Mortimer Adler to help older students learn deep reading strategies for challenging literature, as well as Adler’s _Paideia_ essays for inspiration on Socratic teaching. These foundational books together help newcomers understand _what_ to teach and _why_.
    
- **Classical Curricula Providers:** Several publishing companies now specialize in full curricula and materials for classical education. For example, **Memoria Press** produces an acclaimed, structured classical homeschool curriculum (known for its Latin programs, classical studies, and literature guides). Memoria Press also offers a free mobile app to enhance classical studies – the app provides video courses, audio lectures, and digital flashcards that complement their textbooks . Similarly, **Classical Academic Press** provides curricula for subjects like logic, rhetoric, Latin (their _Song School Latin_ and _Latin for Children_ series use songs and cartoons for younger learners), and they run online classes through Scholé Academy. **Veritas Press** is another provider, known for its **Omnibus** great-books curriculum for secondary students and self-paced online history courses (featuring interactive games and videos about Ancient Egypt, the Middle Ages, etc.). These curricula are designed to be user-friendly for parents schooling at home, with lesson plans and support, while maintaining classical rigor in content.
    
- **Homeschool Communities and Co-ops:** Many families pursue classical education in community with others. **Classical Conversations (CC)** is a nationwide (and international) network of weekly homeschool communities where trained parent-tutors lead groups of children through memory work, science projects, Latin chants, and Socratic discussions. CC alone has over 1,000 communities, reflecting how popular this model has become . Another option is the **Scholé Groups** affiliated with the CiRCE Institute, which emphasize a slower, more reflective “restful” classical education and have 100+ communities . In addition, many churches or local homeschool groups have formed co-ops offering classical classes one or two days a week (sometimes using curricula like those from Memoria Press or Charlotte Mason blends). These groups provide support, accountability, and social opportunities, making the challenging classical curriculum more fun and sustainable for kids.
    
- **Online Academies and Courses:** As mentioned, the **Well-Trained Mind Academy (WTMA)** offers live online courses taught by experienced instructors, in everything from **Latin** and **logic** to **calculus** and **literature**. This allows middle and high school students anywhere in the world to get a classical education experience from home. Classes are kept small and interactive, and the program is accredited as a private online school. Other online programs include **Scholé Academy** (run by Classical Academic Press) and **Wilson Hill Academy**, both of which serve homeschoolers globally with live classical classes for a fee. For younger students or more flexible learning, some parents also use free resources like Khan Academy (for math) or open-course materials from Hillsdale College (which offers free online lectures on history and the Great Books). The move to online learning during the pandemic also saw many classical schools (both private and charter) offering their courses virtually, some of which continue to enroll remote students. For instance, _Great Hearts Online_ extends the Great Hearts classical curriculum to home-based learners with live teachers, and in 2023 it even began offering options for dual enrollment college credit in upper grades .
    
- **Language and Learning Apps:** Despite classical education’s heavy emphasis on books and conversation, technology is also being leveraged to support learning of classical subjects. A good example is the renewed interest in **Latin** – a hallmark of the classical curriculum. In 2019, the popular language app **Duolingo** added a Latin course, making Latin study “free and easy” for anyone with a smartphone . While Duolingo’s Latin course is short (reflecting Latin’s smaller learner base), it signaled that even an ancient language can attract tens of thousands of learners today. Other tools have emerged specifically from classical educators: _Picta Dicta_ is an interactive app that teaches Latin vocabulary through pictures and stories (useful for younger students). **Visual Latin** is a two-year video-based course led by instructor Dwane Thomas, known for its engaging and humorous teaching style – families report that even reluctant students “gravitate towards the screen” because the lessons make them laugh while learning grammar  . The _Visual Latin_ program mixes short videos (available on DVD or streaming) with worksheets and reading practice, and keeps lessons to a manageable length (e.g. 20–30 minutes a day) to hold a child’s interest  . Additionally, many classical curriculum providers incorporate digital supplements: for instance, Memoria Press’ online platform offers flashcard drills and pronunciation audio for Latin forms; and Quizlet flashcard sets are commonly used for memory work (history dates, Latin vocab, etc.) in classical co-ops. There are even games and songs on YouTube for things like Latin noun declensions or history timelines – showing that “child-friendly” approaches can blend with serious content. In essence, while classical education is often low-tech by philosophy (preferring books over screens), modern adaptive learning tools are available to reinforce the material, especially for independent practice in subjects like languages.
    
- **Enrichment Books & Media:** Finally, classical educators today benefit from an abundance of books and media that enrich the learning journey. Historical novels and story-based curriculums like **Susan Wise Bauer’s** own _Story of the World_ (a four-volume world history narrative for elementary students) make subjects like history engaging for young kids. There are also **children’s versions of classics** (for example, _The Odyssey_ retold for kids, or versions of Plato’s dialogues adapted for middle schoolers) that can introduce students to great ideas early on. Families often pursue enriching extracurricular studies as well – for example, using art history books or music recordings to study the great composers (tying into the classical idea of a _holistic_ education including the fine arts). Many classical students take up instruments (reflecting the **quadrivium** art of music) or join speech and debate leagues (practicing rhetoric in action). Even for parents, there are communities like **Circe Institute** or online forums (e.g. the Well-Trained Mind forums) where adults continue their own _Great Conversation_ – discussing the challenges and rewards of educating the next generation in the classical way  .
    

  

In summary, the K–12 classical education movement today is richly supported by both old and new resources. Whether one is a parent seeking a rigorous yet child-friendly homeschool plan, a teacher at a classical school looking for Latin supplements, or an adult interested in the Great Books, there are myriad tools to draw on. Classical education’s resurgence has been fueled by these dedicated individuals (from Sayers and Adler to Bauer and beyond) and organizations that have packaged ancient wisdom for modern learners. Armed with these resources, a growing number of families are rediscovering “the lost tools of learning” and adapting them to educate children who are knowledgeable, articulate, and grounded in timeless ideals of truth, beauty, and goodness.
