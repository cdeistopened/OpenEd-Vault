# ==Technology was never the point==

==Ray Ravaglia—founder of Stanford Online High School and author of== ==["Bricks and Mortar: The Making of a Real Education at the Stanford Online High School"](https://www.amazon.com/Bricks-Mortar-Making-Education-Stanford/dp/1575867397?utm_campaign=14243626-OpenEd%20Daily&utm_source=hs_email&utm_medium=email&_hsenc=p2ANqtz-869Xh7ec4YfcllC5zCm7RR8BzyP1Dm39Yxcv5-8kWtZmH7hvNZ-LeA2UENC5MMcJgQ9Emg)====—learned the hierarchy of what actually matters in education after three decades of building online schools.==

=="It's easy to imagine someone saying,== _=='This school is great, the other kids are so interesting, the teachers are so engaging... the technology crashes every once in a while, but I love this place'==_==," Ray explains.==

=="But you can't imagine the other way where it's like,== _=='Yeah, the other kids are sort of boring, I've seen better teachers, but the immersive video conferencing technology is so compelling, I just want to be in this school==_==."==

==The human factor dominates. Always.==

==Students will tolerate terrible technology if the people are amazing. But perfect technology can't save boring teachers and disengaged classmates.==
