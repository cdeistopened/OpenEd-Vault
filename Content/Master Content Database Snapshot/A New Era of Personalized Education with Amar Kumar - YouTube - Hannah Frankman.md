1. Moving to America for Amar's Education

Amar: I was born in India in what was probably a middle class or upper middle class family. In India, about 70% of kids go to private schools, and I was one of those kids. I went to a very nice private school that was academically rigorous and had good activities and a strong social environment.

However, even those nice schools in India had very limited opportunities after graduation, as India was still a fairly closed economy at the time. As a 10-year-old, I didn't really think about this, but my parents were thinking about the future.

My parents had other family who had immigrated to the United States, and they had a pathway through a visa application. When I was 10 years old, they got that visa, and they decided to make the move. I remember when they told me we were moving to America - I was very confused, as I had never moved anywhere in my life before.

I had this feeling that there was something not good enough about the education in India, and that there was something better in America. I also remember feeling a sense of guilt that I got to leave, while all these other people in India didn't have that same opportunity.

Looking back, I think my parents' decision was driven not just by means, but by a strong awareness of the importance of education. Not every family in India with the means chose to leave, but my parents had the foresight and connections to make that big move for the sake of providing a better educational opportunity.

Even now, as a parent myself, I'm in awe of the decision they made. They had strong communities, careers, and lives in India, but they gave all of that up for this concept of a better education. It's a powerful example of how far parents will go for their children.

1. What Makes American Education System Unique?

When I first arrived in the US and started attending a public elementary school in Chicago, I was very confused. I had just finished 5th grade in India, so I was doing the same material again, and I thought the school system wasn't any better. The kids didn't seem more engaged or interested in learning.

Over time, however, I started to appreciate some of the key differences in the American education system. The biggest one was the focus on application of knowledge, rather than just rote memorization. There was also more of a collaborative, group-oriented approach, versus the more coercive style I was used to in India.

I also had a lot more choice in my subjects and the ability to go deeper in areas I was interested in, like psychology, versus just following a rigid curriculum. These elements helped position me better for making wise decisions about my future career path.

1. When Amar Knew He Was Called to a Career in Education

Looking back, I realize I've always had a calling towards education, even if I didn't recognize it as a viable career path at first. In high school and college, I was always tutoring and serving as a TA. I loved that spark I would see in someone's eyes when they finally grasped a concept.

However, I never seriously considered teaching as a profession, because it didn't fit the typical paths of engineering or medicine that were expected in my family. It wasn't until a few years into working as a computer scientist at a pharmaceutical company that I really started to feel unfulfilled.

I had this nagging sense that I should be doing more, that I was missing that burning desire I felt I should have in my 20s. That's when I realized my true calling was in teaching. I decided to take a sabbatical and move back to India to teach math at a high school there for a year.

That experience was incredibly eye-opening. I saw the "Swiss cheese" problem firsthand, where students were at vastly different skill levels all sitting in the same classroom. Trying to get them all prepared for their high-stakes exit exams was an immense challenge. But it also showed me that the issue wasn't just with that one school - it was a systemic problem across the education system.

That's when I knew I couldn't go back to computer programming. I had to find a way to drive systemic change in education, not just help one classroom or one school. This pivotal experience lit the fire that would eventually lead me to launching KaiPod.

1. Creating Systemic Change in Education

After that transformative experience teaching in India, I knew I couldn't go back to a computer science career. I was determined to drive systemic change in education, not just impact one classroom or school.

Initially, I thought the path was to get a PhD in education and become an expert. But everyone I spoke to said that was the wrong approach if I truly wanted to create widespread change. They advised me that I needed an MBA to understand how to drive organizational transformation.

So that's the path I pursued - getting an MBA and then joining McKinsey's education practice, where I could advise school districts and universities on large-scale initiatives. However, I started to feel frustrated that most of the work was just creating slide decks and telling others what to do, rather than getting my hands dirty with implementation.

I then moved to Pearson, the world's largest education company, to lead a major digital transformation. This gave me insight into the power of flexible, online learning models. But I still wasn't satisfied - I wanted to see the direct impact in classrooms.

Within Pearson, I shifted to leading their online school division, which served students learning from home. This exposed me to the unique challenges and benefits of online learning, and the need to pair it with in-person community and support.

1. The Genesis of KaiPod

The COVID-19 pandemic was a pivotal moment that really crystallized my vision for KaiPod. Seeing how many families thrived with the flexibility of online learning, yet struggled with the isolation, made me realize there was a huge unmet need.

I hypothesized that the "pandemic pods" emerging would be more than a temporary trend - that parents were going to increasingly demand the ability to choose their child's learning model, pairing online curriculum with in-person community.

So in September 2021, in the midst of the pandemic, I launched the first KaiPod location in Boston. We started with a small pilot of just 9 students, but even in that short trial, we saw amazing results. The kids were making 3 times the progress of a traditional classroom, and they were raving about the experience.

However, the pilot also surfaced an important insight - while the model worked incredibly well, it was still too foreign for many families to fully embrace, as they were so accustomed to the traditional school model. So we had to figure out how to make the transition more approachable.

1. How Does KaiPod Function?

Initially, our plan for KaiPod was for all students to use the same online curriculum. But as we interviewed and recruited families, we realized they had very strong opinions about the specific curricula they wanted their kids to use - curricula they had carefully selected after years of trying alternatives.

So we pivoted our approach to be completely open to whatever curriculum the family wanted to use. Our role as KaiPod is to provide the in-person learning coaches, community, and support - not to dictate the academic content.

This was a source of terror for us at first, as we realized we could have 10 kids using 10 different programs, and the learning coach would have to figure out how to support each one. But it has actually worked incredibly well.

Fundamentally, we believe that parents know best about how their child learns and which curricula are working. Our job is to respect that choice, provide guidance and opinions, but ultimately empower the families to select what's right for them.

In our KaiPod locations, you'll find kids using a diverse array of math, English, and other academic platforms. Sometimes the kids and parents will even discover new options by seeing what others are using. It creates a "market effect" within the classroom.

1. Allowing for Specificity in Education

One of the core beliefs that has emerged for us at KaiPod is that parents deserve choice in what and how their child is learning. This can sometimes be interpreted in a political way, but our intent is simply that parents know best about their child's unique needs and learning style.

We have a lot of recommendations and opinions, and if we see a curriculum really isn't working for a child, we feel obligated to tell the family. But ultimately, the decision rests with the parents. We respect their right to choose the path that's best for their child.

This philosophy of allowing for specificity is a key differentiator of the KaiPod model. Rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all approach, we've embraced the complexity of having kids on diverse learning paths within the same physical space.

Logistically, this does require our learning coaches to have a high level of expertise. We intentionally hire former classroom teachers who are accustomed to juggling multiple needs and lesson plans simultaneously. It takes them about 6-8 weeks to get fully ramped up on supporting all the different curricula.

But the end result is a beautiful partnership between the parent, coach, and child - where they're all working together towards the same goals, with the child in the learning model that works best for them.

1. How Did the Pandemic Disrupt the Homeschool Movement?

The COVID-19 pandemic was a major disruption that actually accelerated interest in alternative education models, while also decelerated the progress of the homeschool movement in some ways.

I find it interesting to talk to the parents who are the most traumatized by the pandemic experience - not the ones who are seeking out alternatives, but the ones who are most adamant about sending their kids back to traditional school the first day it reopens.

For many of these parents, the sudden isolation and regression they saw in their kids during remote learning created a deep aversion. They don't ever want to think about that experience again. The trauma of being thrown into it so abruptly, without support, and then facing judgment, was incredibly difficult.

On the flip side, the families I interact with at KaiPod are the ones who saw a silver lining in the pandemic. They realized their kids thrived with the flexibility of online learning and the freedom from things like bullying. They're now seeking options that maintain that personalization, while also addressing the social-emotional needs that remote learning couldn't fulfill.

So the pandemic really exposed how critical that social component is - it's not just about childcare, but the consistent community and peer interaction that traditional schools provide. And that's a key part of what KaiPod aims to deliver.

1. Who is Public School Right For?

Personally, I don't believe the traditional classroom model is right for any child - whether it's in a public, private, or microschool setting. I think that model of 30 kids in a class is fundamentally flawed and not conducive to how humans actually learn.

However, I do think there is a place for public schools if they can adapt to more of a microschooling approach - using technology to enhance learning, having teachers act more as coaches than lecturers, and providing flexible pathways. The challenge is that public systems aren't set up to make that kind of dramatic shift.

So in my view, the public school system is best suited for the kids for whom that standardized model actually does work - the ones who thrive in a more one-size-fits-all environment. But I believe those students are the minority. The majority of kids would benefit far more from the personalization and flexibility of alternative models.

My goal with KaiPod is not to compete with or replace public schools, but to provide a wonderful landing pad for the families who are jumping off that "Ferris wheel" of a broken system. We want to build microschools in every community that can serve those families with choice, personalization, and community.

1. How Does an ESA work?

When people talk about the concern that alternative education models are pulling public funding away, I have a few key responses. First, you're not "pulling" public dollars out of the system - those are local tax dollars that belong to the families, not the government school district.

What Education Savings Accounts (ESAs) allow is for families to direct that money to the education option that best fits their child, rather than it defaulting to the government-run school. I'd love to see a grand experiment where an entire school district gives ESAs to every single family, and lets them choose where to take that funding - whether it's the local public school, a private school, a microschool, or something else.

Ultimately, I believe families should be the ones actively making that choice, rather than it being the default that their money sits with a government-run school that may no longer be serving their needs. Just because the mayor runs the school district doesn't mean they have any more business running a school than I do running a microschool.

The second response is that the concern about families with means leaving and low-income families being stuck in a broken system - that's an education problem, not a choice problem. There are amazing organizations working to educate families on their options and empower them to make the best choices.

1. Scaling KaiPod

As we've rapidly expanded KaiPod to 5 states with partners in 25 more, I've really come to see how microschooling can be a "red, blue, urban, rural" strategy. It's the education model that I believe can truly serve every community and demographic.

The markets we operate in fall into two main archetypes. In places like Phoenix, families are incredibly educated consumers of education - they're extremely picky and discerning, and we have to deliver an absolutely top-notch experience to earn their business. It's like running a restaurant in New York City.

Then in other markets, like our first location in Boston, parents aren't as used to having choice. The traditional public/private school bundles are all they know. So we've had to be more gradual in how we introduce the flexibility and modularity of the KaiPod model, to get them comfortable with the concept of picking and choosing.

Regardless of the market, we see an incredible hunger from families for this type of personalized, community-driven education. The challenge is often just helping them make the mental leap beyond the two-bundle reality they're used to. But once they do, they become incredible advocates for the KaiPod approach.

1. Why is Education So Slow to Evolve?

I believe there are a few key reasons why education has been so slow to evolve, especially compared to other industries transformed by technology and the internet.

First, I think there's a perverse incentive at play, where the smartest graduates are drawn to high-paying jobs at tech companies and social media giants, rather than going into education or other public service fields. If I was an alien civilization trying to destroy the Earth, I'd do whatever I could to pull the best talent away from schools and hospitals.

The second reason is that large systems, like school districts, simply can't change without competitive pressure. Cable companies didn't evolve until streaming services emerged. Hospitals don't improve outcomes until there's competition in a community. Education has lacked that competitive dynamic, with the public school system being a government-backed monopoly.

But the rise of Education Savings Accounts is changing that playing field. Now, microschools and alternative providers can compete on a level playing field, rather than always being at a disadvantage to the "free" public option. This competitive pressure is what I believe will finally force the large systems to evolve.
